The Ministry of Defence issues policy circulars through the Department of Defence under the authority of the Raksha Mantri, and all such circulars pertaining to service matters of defence personnel are assigned a unique reference number following the standardised format MoD/[Section Code]/[Year]/D([Department Code]), ensuring traceability and administrative accountability across all levels of the armed forces hierarchy. Upon formal issuance, each circular is transmitted to all Commands, Area Headquarters, Sub-Area Headquarters, and Station Headquarters through secure official channels, which may include the Military Communication Network, encrypted electronic mail on the NIC defence domain, and physical dispatch through the confidential dak system where electronic transmission is not feasible. The Department of Defence Production, the Department of Defence Research and Development, and the Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare each receive copies of circulars that pertain to their respective functional domains, and the Integrated Defence Staff Headquarters is simultaneously kept in the loop for all matters having inter-services implications. When a circular supersedes a previous instrument, the new circular explicitly states the reference number and date of the document being superseded, along with the effective date from which the earlier circular ceases to have operational validity. Circulars issued under the authority of the Chief of the Army Staff, the Chief of the Naval Staff, or the Chief of the Air Staff in their respective service-specific matters bear a distinct prefix denoting the issuing Service Headquarters, such as AHQ, NHQ, or AFHQ, followed by the relevant branch code and serial number for that financial year. All policy circulars are maintained in a master register at the Policy Directorate of the respective Service Headquarters, and a consolidated index is published at the end of each financial year for reference by all administrative authorities. Amendments to existing circulars are issued as Corrigenda bearing the same reference number as the parent circular with an appended suffix, such as /Corr-1 or /Amdt-2, so as to ensure that the amendment is traceable to the original instrument without creating ambiguity in its interpretation. Formation Commanders of the rank of General Officer Commanding and above are authorised to issue administrative instructions within their area of jurisdiction, provided such instructions do not conflict with any extant MoD circular or Army/Navy/Air Force order, and any such local instructions must be forwarded to Army Headquarters for record within thirty days of issuance. The Directorate of Military Secretary, in coordination with the Adjutant General's Branch, reviews all circulars relating to personnel policy on a five-yearly cycle or whenever a Pay Commission recommendation necessitates revision, and any circular found to be outdated or inconsistent with current statutory provisions is formally withdrawn through a cancellation notice bearing its own reference number. All defence personnel are expected to be familiar with circulars relevant to their appointment, and ignorance of a duly promulgated circular is not accepted as a defence in proceedings under the Army Act 1950, the Navy Act 1957, or the Air Force Act 1950.

The entitlement of annual leave for officers of the Indian Army, Navy, and Air Force is governed by the Leave Rules for Officers as promulgated under the authority of the respective Service Headquarters and as periodically updated through MoD policy circulars. Officers of the rank of Lieutenant to Colonel are entitled to sixty days of annual leave per calendar year, which may be availed in one or more instalments subject to the exigencies of service and the approval of the Commanding Officer or the next superior authority as applicable to the posting held by the officer. Officers of the rank of Brigadier and above are entitled to avail annual leave with the approval of the Army Commander or equivalent authority, and the leave must be planned in such a manner as not to adversely affect the operational readiness of the formation. Junior Commissioned Officers, which include the ranks of Naib Subedar, Subedar, and Subedar Major in the Army and their equivalents in the Navy and Air Force, are entitled to thirty days of annual leave per year, and the application for such leave must be submitted on the prescribed proforma at least fifteen days in advance except in cases of urgency duly certified by the unit Medical Officer. Other Ranks, comprising Havildars, Naiks, Lance Naiks, and Sepoys and their equivalents in sister services, are entitled to thirty days of annual leave per calendar year, and the sanctioning authority for such leave is the Commanding Officer of the unit. Casual leave is an entitlement separate from annual leave and is limited to ten days per calendar year for all ranks, and it is not encashable nor can it be accumulated from one calendar year to the next, and it is intended to cover short absences for personal and domestic reasons. The application for casual leave is to be made verbally or in writing to the immediate superior authority and requires only informal approval, with a record being maintained in the unit leave register by the Adjutant or the equivalent administrative officer of the unit. Special leave is granted in specific circumstances as prescribed by policy, including leave for participation in sports events of national or international standing, leave for undertaking recognised courses of higher education, and leave for attending to family obligations such as the marriage of a first-degree relative, and such leave is not debited to the annual leave account. Leave not availed during a calendar year may be accumulated up to a maximum of three hundred days for officers and one hundred and fifty days for other ranks over the entirety of their service, and the accumulated leave balance at the time of retirement or release from service is encashed at the rate of daily emoluments as defined under the applicable pay rules. Any leave availed in excess of entitlement without prior sanction is treated as unauthorised absence and is liable to result in disciplinary proceedings under the provisions of the relevant Service Act, and such absence is also reflected adversely in the Annual Confidential Report of the individual concerned.

Medical leave for defence personnel is governed by the provisions of Regulation 423 of the Regulations for the Army (Part I) and the corresponding provisions of Naval and Air Force Regulations, and it is distinct from annual or casual leave in that it is certified by a medical authority and debited to a separate account maintained at the unit level. When a defence person is admitted to a Military Hospital or a civil hospital empanelled under the Ex-Servicemen Contributory Health Scheme or the Ayushman Bharat Defence scheme, the period of hospitalisation is treated as duty for all purposes including pay and allowances, and no leave is debited during the period of actual inpatient treatment. Upon discharge from hospital, the Medical Officer of the Military Hospital issues a Medical Board Proceedings Form, which includes the diagnosis, the treatment administered, and the recommended period of convalescence, and the Commanding Officer of the unit is required to grant convalescent leave for the period so recommended without requiring the individual to apply separately. Extension of medical leave beyond the initially sanctioned period requires a fresh recommendation from a Board of Medical Officers at the station where the individual is availing leave, and the extension is to be communicated to the unit through the Station Medical Authority, with a copy endorsed to the Record Office of the individual. In cases where the illness is of a prolonged or permanent nature affecting the individual's fitness for military service, a Release Medical Board or an Invaliding Board may be convened by the appropriate Command Medical Authority, and the findings of such a board are forwarded to the Directorate General of Armed Forces Medical Services for review and final orders. The effect of prolonged medical leave on the service record of an officer or JCO is regulated such that medical leave in excess of one year in any period of three years may adversely affect the calculation of qualifying service for pension, and such cases are individually reviewed by the Pension Sanctioning Authority at the Office of the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts. Medical leave availed on account of injuries sustained in the performance of duty, including battle casualties and injuries during exercise or training, does not count against the individual's leave account and is treated as duty throughout, provided a certificate of the causal connection between the injury and service duty is issued by the Commanding Officer. Cases of alcohol-induced illness or self-inflicted injuries are treated differently, and medical leave arising from such circumstances is governed by special provisions that may result in the leave being treated as a period without pay, subject to the findings of a Court of Inquiry ordered by the Commanding Officer. Defence personnel on medical leave are not permitted to engage in any form of remunerative employment during the period of such leave, and violation of this condition may lead to disciplinary action under the relevant Service Act. All medical leave records are maintained at the Record Office of the respective Corps or Regiment and are taken into consideration at the time of promotion boards, extension of service, and finalisation of pension entitlements.

The transfer and posting policy of the Ministry of Defence, as applicable to the Indian Army, is governed by the provisions of MoD circular MoD/MS/2019/D(AG) and subsequent amendments thereto, and lays down the minimum tenure norms, the criteria for peace and field classification of stations, and the authorities competent to effect postings at various levels of rank. Field stations are classified as stations located in operational areas, high-altitude areas above a specified altitude of three thousand metres, counter-insurgency areas notified by the Ministry of Home Affairs, and remote areas where family accommodation is not generally available, and the tenure for such stations is fixed at two years, extendable to three years with the consent of the individual. Peace stations are all other stations where family accommodation is available and normal civil amenities are accessible, and the minimum tenure for peace stations is two years for officers and three years for JCOs and other ranks, and premature transfer from a peace station is ordinarily not effected unless warranted by exigencies of service or a specific administrative requirement certified by the formation commander. Officers of the rank of Colonel and above are posted by the Military Secretary's Branch of Army Headquarters, while postings of officers of the rank of Lieutenant Colonel and below are effected by the respective Command Headquarters in coordination with the Directorate General of the Corps or Arm concerned. Postings of JCOs and other ranks are handled by the respective Record Offices under the administrative supervision of the Adjutant General's Branch, and such postings are regulated by the provisions of the Army Order on Postings issued periodically by Army Headquarters. Family Separation Allowance is payable to defence personnel posted to field or operational areas or to stations where family accommodation is not allotted, at rates prescribed by the Sixth and Seventh Central Pay Commission recommendations as implemented through Government of India, Ministry of Finance orders, and the allowance is credited monthly along with the pay and allowances of the individual. Hardship posting areas, which include the Siachen Glacier, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, and certain border areas along the Line of Actual Control and Line of Control, carry additional compensatory allowances such as the Siachen Allowance, the Island Special Duty Allowance, and the High Altitude Allowance, as applicable to the category of the posting. Personnel posted to hardship areas are given a priority in subsequent posting preferences, and the Military Secretary's Branch maintains a posting preference register in which the individual's stated preference is recorded and considered subject to service exigencies. The policy also provides that personnel shall not be posted to a station within fifty kilometres of the station from which a member of their immediate family, serving in the same arm or service, has been recently transferred, so as to prevent concentration of family members in a single geographic area. Appeals against posting orders may be made to the next superior posting authority within thirty days of receipt of the posting order, and such appeals are considered only on medical, humanitarian, or operational grounds and not as a matter of right.

The criteria for promotion of Junior Commissioned Officers in the Indian Army are governed by the provisions of the Army Act 1950 and the corresponding Regulations, supplemented by Army Headquarters Policy Letters issued periodically by the Adjutant General's Branch in coordination with the Military Secretary's Branch. For promotion from Naib Subedar to Subedar, the minimum service requirement is five years in the lower rank, and the individual must have a clean service record with no red ink entries, which refer to adverse remarks, punishments, or Court of Inquiry findings recorded against the individual. The promotion of Subedar to Subedar Major is governed by seniority cum merit, meaning that while seniority in service is the primary criterion, the quality of Annual Confidential Report gradings over the preceding five years is a decisive factor, and a JCO with an overall grading of below average in any one of the five years preceding the promotion board is ordinarily not considered. The Annual Confidential Report for JCOs is initiated by the Company Commander of the rank of Captain or Major, reviewed by the Commanding Officer of the rank of Colonel, and the final grading is awarded by the Reviewing Officer, who is the Brigade Commander or the equivalent formation commander. The Regimental Selection Board, which is the primary selection authority for JCO promotions, meets annually or as directed by Army Headquarters, and it comprises the Commanding Officer as Chairman and the senior-most JCO of the unit as a special invitee, and its findings are forwarded to the Record Office for implementation. For officers, the promotion from Lieutenant to Captain is time-bound and is effected after two years of commissioned service, subject to the officer not having any disciplinary case pending. Promotion from Captain to Major is effected after six years of commissioned service subject to satisfactory ACR grading, while promotion from Major to Lieutenant Colonel, from Lieutenant Colonel to Colonel, and from Colonel to Brigadier is made by the Army Headquarters Selection Board, which meets annually and considers officers within the specified Zone of Consideration, defined as the batch of officers who are due for promotion in the current or immediately preceding year. The Zone of Consideration rules are strictly applied, and officers who are superseded for promotion twice at any rank are ordinarily not considered again for promotion to that rank, and they may be required to seek premature retirement in accordance with the provisions applicable to their arm or service. Merit over seniority is the guiding principle for selection to the ranks of Brigadier and above, and the Selection Board records its findings in a graded list from which the recommended candidates are drawn in order of merit as assessed by the board. Officers with outstanding or very high gradings in their ACRs, decorated for gallantry, or holding key staff or command appointments are given additional weightage during board proceedings, and the final promotion list is placed before the Raksha Mantri for approval before being notified through Army Headquarters orders.

The allotment of married accommodation to defence personnel is governed by the Married Accommodation Project policy and the extant orders of the respective Service Headquarters, and the entitlement of a defence person to married accommodation is based on the rank held by the individual and the availability of accommodation of the appropriate type at the station of posting. Accommodation is classified into type designations ranging from Type I to Type VII, with Type I and Type II being allotted to Other Ranks and Junior Commissioned Officers respectively, Types III and IV to officers of the rank of Captain to Colonel, and Types V, VI, and VII to officers of the rank of Brigadier and above. The priority list for accommodation allotment is maintained by the Station Accommodation Officer at each station, and the priority is determined by the date of joining the station and the date of application, with modifications for personnel posted to the station from field or operational areas who are accorded higher priority as per the provisions of the Station Standing Orders. The waiting period for married accommodation varies from station to station depending on availability, and in stations such as Delhi Cantonment, Pune, and Bangalore, the waiting period may extend from six months to two years owing to the high density of defence units and the limited number of available quarters. Personnel who have been allotted accommodation are required to take physical possession within fifteen days of receipt of the allotment order, failing which the allotment is liable to be cancelled and the next person on the priority list is considered. Unauthorised occupation of married accommodation, which includes occupying a quarter beyond the authorised retention period, occupying a quarter of a type higher than entitlement without formal allotment, or sub-letting accommodation to civilians, is a serious misconduct and attracts action under the provisions of the Cantonment Act 2006 and the relevant provisions of the Service Act, in addition to recovery of damages at market rental rates. Upon transfer from a station, the allottee is required to vacate the married accommodation within thirty days of the date of relief from the posting, and an extension up to sixty days may be granted by the Station Commander on humanitarian grounds such as the ongoing academic session of school-going children. The Station Accommodation Officer is empowered to initiate eviction proceedings through the Cantonment Magistrate in cases of refusal to vacate, and the cost of such proceedings is recoverable from the individual concerned. Retired defence personnel, including those on re-employment, are not entitled to married accommodation in military cantonment areas except as specifically provided under the re-employment rules, and their retention of quarters beyond the permissible period is actionable under the Cantonment Act. All complaints relating to the condition of married accommodation, rectification of defects, and civil maintenance are to be addressed to the Military Engineering Services through the Station Accommodation Officer and not directly to the Cantonment Board.

Children Education Allowance for defence personnel is sanctioned under the provisions of Government of India, Ministry of Finance Office Memorandum No. 12011/03/2008-Estt.(Allowance) as subsequently adopted and modified by the Ministry of Defence for armed forces personnel, and is subject to revision as and when the Central Pay Commission recommendations are accepted and implemented by the Government of India. The allowance is payable for a maximum of two children per defence person, irrespective of the total number of children in the family, and is admissible from Class I to Class XII of recognised schools, including Kendriya Vidyalayas, Jawahar Navodaya Vidyalayas, Army Public Schools, and any school recognised by a State Board, the Central Board of Secondary Education, the Indian Certificate of Secondary Education, or equivalent national-level education authority. The maximum reimbursable amount per child per annum was fixed at twenty-seven thousand rupees per child per year under the recommendations of the Seventh Central Pay Commission, and this amount is subject to revision upon acceptance of the Eighth Pay Commission recommendations which are currently under consideration as of the financial year 2024-25. The reimbursement procedure requires the defence person to submit the original fee receipts issued by the school, along with a certificate from the school confirming the enrolment and attendance of the child, to the unit accounts section or the Pay Accounts Office of the respective service within three months of the end of the academic year for which reimbursement is sought. The Paying Authority, which is the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts or the Controller of Defence Accounts as appropriate to the station, examines the claim for admissibility and sanctions the reimbursement within thirty working days of receipt of a complete claim with all supporting documents. The allowance is not payable if the child is availing a full scholarship covering tuition and other fees, and in cases of partial scholarship, only the net amount actually paid by the parent is reimbursable subject to the prescribed ceiling. Hostel subsidy is separately admissible for children studying in residential institutions at stations other than the parent's place of posting, and the rate of hostel subsidy is fixed at a separate ceiling as notified by the Ministry of Finance and adopted by MoD. Personnel posted to field areas where schools for children are not available are given additional consideration under the policy, and the reimbursement procedure is simplified for such personnel to reduce the administrative burden of submitting claims from remote locations. The Children Education Allowance is not to be confused with the Educational Concession available to the wards of Armed Forces personnel studying in Sainik Schools, which is a separate concession governed by the Sainik Schools Society and the Ministry of Defence. All claims for Children Education Allowance must be certified by the Commanding Officer before submission to the Pay Accounts Office, and any false certification by a defence person is a disciplinary offence liable to action under the Army Act, Navy Act, or Air Force Act as applicable.

Compassionate appointment in Government service for the dependants of deceased or disabled defence personnel is governed by the provisions of MoD policy circular No. MoD/D(Pen/Policy)/2017/232 and the Department of Personnel and Training guidelines on compassionate appointment as applicable to defence establishments. The scheme applies to the dependants of defence personnel who die in harness, including death attributable to service, and also to the dependants of personnel who are invalided out of service on account of disability attributable to or aggravated by military service, thereby rendering the family without a source of regular income. Eligible dependants include the spouse, sons, and daughters of the deceased or disabled defence person, and in the absence of an immediately eligible spouse or children, the scheme may extend to dependent parents, subject to the fulfilment of the means test prescribed in the policy. The means test requires that the total family income, including pension, family pension, interest on savings, and any other regular source, does not exceed a prescribed threshold, which is periodically revised, and any family whose income exceeds this threshold is not eligible for compassionate appointment irrespective of the circumstances of the death or disability. The application for compassionate appointment is to be submitted to the Record Office of the respective Corps or Regiment within five years of the death or invaliding of the defence person, and applications received after this period are not considered except in extraordinary circumstances as determined by the Director General Resettlement. The Record Office examines the application for completeness and admissibility and forwards it to the Ministry of Defence, Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare, which coordinates with the concerned Ministry or Department of the Central Government or the relevant State Government for identification of a suitable post. Compassionate appointment is ordinarily made to Group C posts in Government of India establishments, and the appointee must possess the minimum educational qualifications prescribed for the post, failing which the appointment cannot be made. The waiting period before a compassionate appointment is made varies depending on the availability of vacancies and the administrative process, and it may extend from six months to two years in many cases. Personnel appointed on compassionate grounds are treated as fresh recruits for all service purposes and do not carry forward any service benefits or seniority from the deceased or disabled parent's service. The Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare maintains a centralised register of all pending compassionate appointment cases and furnishes a quarterly report to the Raksha Rajya Mantri on the status of disposal of such applications.

Discipline and conduct of defence personnel are regulated under the Army Act 1950, the Navy Act 1957, the Air Force Act 1950, and the respective service regulations, which together constitute the legal and administrative framework for maintaining order, efficiency, and integrity within the armed forces. Misconduct under these provisions includes, but is not limited to, disobedience of lawful commands, insubordination, absence without leave, fraud upon the Government, conduct unbecoming of an officer or a gentleman, drunkenness while on duty, and any act prejudicial to good order and military discipline as determined by the Commanding Officer. The Commanding Officer of a unit holds primary responsibility for the discipline of all personnel under his command and is empowered to award summary punishments for minor offences in the case of other ranks, including confinement to barracks, extra duties, and stoppage of pay, subject to the limits prescribed in the relevant schedule of the Army Act. For more serious offences, the Commanding Officer is required to refer the matter to a Court of Inquiry, which is a fact-finding body constituted under Rule 177 of the Army Rules 1954, and the findings of the Court of Inquiry form the basis for initiation of further proceedings such as a Summary of Evidence or a Court Martial as appropriate to the gravity of the offence. Offences committed by officers are reported by the Commanding Officer to the Brigade Commander, who in turn forwards the matter to the General Officer Commanding the Division or equivalent formation for decision on the nature of proceedings to be initiated. The Annual Confidential Report of any individual against whom disciplinary proceedings have been initiated must reflect the fact of such proceedings in the relevant column, and until the proceedings are concluded and the individual is either honourably acquitted or awarded a punishment, the ACR gradings for that period are withheld and cannot be used for promotion consideration. A conviction by a General Court Martial may result in the cashiering or dismissal of an officer, and such officers are debarred from receiving pension or gratuity unless the President of India specifically directs otherwise on compassionate grounds. Red ink entries in the service record of a JCO or other rank, which record punishments formally awarded under the Army Act, affect eligibility for promotion, award of good conduct badges, and computation of service for pension. The role of the Judge Advocate General's Branch is to provide legal advice to commanding officers and formation commanders on matters of discipline, and the Branch reviews all Court Martial proceedings to ensure compliance with the procedural requirements of the Army Act and Rules. The spirit of the Code of Conduct as laid down in the Regulations for the Army emphasises that every defence person is expected to uphold the honour of the uniform, the traditions of the regiment, and the constitutional obligation of allegiance to the Republic of India in all personal and professional conduct.

Uniform and dress regulations for the Indian Army are contained in the Dress Regulations for the Army, promulgated under the authority of the Chief of the Army Staff, with the most recent comprehensive revision having been issued as Dress Regulations for the Army 2009, subsequently updated through a series of Army Headquarters letters and corrigenda. Every officer and soldier is entitled to receive the initial outfit of uniform and equipment at Government expense upon commissioning or enrolment, and thereafter the maintenance of uniform is the personal responsibility of the individual, with monetary compensation provided through the Kit Maintenance Allowance sanctioned under the Seventh Pay Commission recommendations. The categories of dress prescribed under the regulations include Service Dress, Mess Dress, Combat Dress, Barrack Dress, and Ceremonial Dress, each of which is appropriate to specific occasions and governed by detailed instructions on the accoutrements to be worn with each category. Service Dress is the day uniform worn in office environments, during official parades, and in formal duty settings, and it consists of the prescribed jacket, trousers or skirt, shirt, cap, and belt, with rank badges, unit and arm badges, and authorised decorations in their prescribed positions. Mess Dress is the formal evening uniform worn at regimental dinners and formal social functions, and it consists of the miniature pattern of jacket, waistcoat, and trousers in the regimental colours, with miniature medals, and its specifications differ regiment by regiment as per the historical tradition of the unit. Modifications to the prescribed uniform, including unauthorised changes to colour, cut, badge placement, or insignia, are a violation of dress regulations and are subject to disciplinary action, and Commanding Officers are responsible for ensuring compliance with dress regulations through regular inspections. The most significant recent change to Army dress regulations was the introduction of the New Pattern Combat Uniform in Multi-Terrain Pattern camouflage in 2012, replacing the older Disruptive Pattern Material uniform, and this change was implemented across all arms and services in a phased manner over two years. Rank badges, name tags, and qualification badges are to be worn only in their authorised positions as specified in the Dress Regulations, and the wearing of unauthorised qualification badges or foreign awards without the express sanction of the Government of India is prohibited. The wearing of civilian clothes while on duty in uniform or while travelling in uniform to or from duty is regulated, and defence personnel appearing in public in a manner unbecoming of the uniform may be subject to disciplinary action by the nearest military authority. Dress Regulations are equally applicable to women officers and women soldiers of the Corps of Military Police and other arms as applicable, and specific provisions for the design and fit of female uniforms have been notified through separate Army Headquarters instructions issued periodically.

Canteen and mess entitlements for defence personnel are governed by the provisions of the Army Canteen Services regulations, administered by the Canteen Stores Department under the Ministry of Defence, and the applicable provisions of the mess regulations of each arm and service. All Other Ranks and Junior Commissioned Officers are entitled to purchase their monthly quota of authorised items from the unit canteen managed by the Canteen Stores Department at concessional prices that are lower than the prevailing market price, and the entitlement quantities for items such as rations, toilet articles, beverages, and textiles are fixed in the Canteen Entitlement Schedule notified by the Canteen Stores Department and updated periodically. Officers are entitled to purchase from the Officers' Mess Account and from the Canteen Stores Department at the rates applicable to their rank group, and the entitlement schedule for officers differs in quantity and category from that of JCOs and other ranks. The Mess Committee, comprising elected representatives of officers in the station or unit, is responsible for the administration of the Officers' Mess including management of the mess fund, approval of the monthly mess bill, and maintenance of the mess inventory, and its accounts are subject to audit by the Controller of Defence Accounts. The mess bill for each officer is calculated monthly and includes the fixed charges for accommodation and services, the proportionate share of the entertainment account, and the personal account charges for items individually consumed, and the mess bill is debited to the officer's pay account through a mandatory recovery authorised by the Mess Secretary. During field postings and operational deployments, the mess and canteen entitlements are modified by the field scale of rations which is higher in calories and nutrition to account for the additional physical demands of operational service, and the field ration scale is issued under authority of the Quarter Master General's Branch in accordance with MoD-approved scales. Canteen facility is not ordinarily available at forward posts above a specified altitude, and in such cases, the entitlement is converted into a cash allowance at rates fixed by the Canteen Stores Department and credited monthly to the pay of the entitled individual. The authority to approve deviations from the standard canteen entitlement schedule in operational areas is vested in the General Officer Commanding the Division or the Brigade Commander in the case of independent brigades. Misuse of canteen entitlements, including re-sale of canteen articles to civilians or purchasing on behalf of ineligible persons, is a disciplinary offence and is subject to action under the relevant service act in addition to cancellation of canteen privileges. The Canteen Stores Department conducts quarterly checks of unit canteen accounts and stock registers, and irregularities found during such checks are reported to the Station Commander and the formation headquarters for administrative and disciplinary action.

The policy governing the use of mobile phones and electronic devices by defence personnel within military establishments is promulgated through Army Headquarters Policy Letter No. AHQ/AG/MP/Policy/2018/4321 and the corresponding service instructions of the Indian Navy and Indian Air Force, and it classifies all military establishments and their precincts into three zones for the purpose of regulating electronic device usage. Zone I, designated as a Prohibited Area, encompasses all operational areas, signal centres, intelligence establishments, classified briefing rooms, command operations centres, and restricted areas notified by the Station Commander, and the possession of any mobile phone, tablet, smart watch, or electronic device capable of communication or data storage is strictly prohibited within Zone I at all times. Zone II, designated as a Controlled Area, covers the remaining areas within the military cantonment or unit lines, and the use of mobile phones in this zone is permitted for personal communication during off-duty hours, but devices with camera capability are not to be used for photography of any military installation, equipment, or formation, and the use of social media applications on such devices is governed by the separate Social Media Policy of the respective service. Zone III covers areas outside the cantonment boundary where defence personnel may use mobile phones freely, subject to the general restrictions on operational security and the prohibition on disclosing service-related information on public platforms. Personnel found in possession of electronic devices in Zone I are subject to immediate disciplinary action under the provisions of the Official Secrets Act 1923 in addition to the applicable Service Act, and the devices so found are liable to be confiscated pending inquiry. The Commanding Officer is responsible for ensuring the enforcement of the mobile phone policy within the unit lines and for briefing all personnel including newly joined officers and soldiers on the provisions of the policy within thirty days of their joining. Specific provisions apply to the use of Global Positioning System-enabled devices, drone cameras, and remote-sensing equipment, and such devices may not be brought into any military cantonment without prior written approval from the Station Commander endorsed by the Intelligence Officer of the station. Service personnel are not permitted to connect personal devices to any official computer network, server, or communication system, and violation of this restriction attracts action under the Information Technology Act 2000 in addition to the relevant service legislation. The formation intelligence officer is designated as the nodal officer for monitoring compliance with the electronic device policy and for reporting violations to the formation commander, and a monthly compliance report is to be forwarded to the Command Intelligence Officer. The policy is reviewed annually by the Directorate of Military Intelligence in consultation with the respective service headquarters and updated as necessary to address emerging threats from advances in communication and surveillance technology.

The procedure governing foreign travel by serving defence personnel is laid down in MoD circular No. MoD/D(MS)/2016/Policy/1847 dated April 14, 2016, and subsequent amendments, and it requires that all foreign travel, whether official or private, must be cleared by the competent authority before the defence person undertakes such travel. For private foreign travel, officers of the rank of Lieutenant to Colonel are required to submit an application on the prescribed form to the Military Secretary's Branch through their Commanding Officer at least sixty days before the intended date of travel, and the application must include details of the countries to be visited, the purpose of travel, the duration of stay, and the source of funding for the travel. Officers of the rank of Brigadier and above, and all officers in sensitive appointments as notified by the Directorate of Military Intelligence, are required to obtain the personal approval of the Chief of the Army Staff or the equivalent Service Chief for private foreign travel to any country other than Bhutan and Nepal. Certain countries are classified as Restricted Countries for the purpose of this policy, and these include countries with which India does not maintain formal diplomatic relations and countries identified as state sponsors of terrorism or as adversaries of India's strategic interests, and travel to such countries is ordinarily not permitted except for officially mandated visits. The passport of a serving defence person is held in the custody of the unit or station headquarters, and it is released to the individual only upon formal sanction of foreign travel, and the individual is required to return the passport to the custody of the headquarters within seventy-two hours of return from abroad. For official foreign travel, including participation in joint exercises, defence attache visits, official delegations, and training programmes in friendly foreign countries, the clearance is obtained at the level of the respective Service Headquarters with endorsement from the Ministry of External Affairs where required. Personnel who fail to obtain prior clearance for foreign travel or who travel to restricted countries without authorisation are subject to disciplinary action under the relevant service act, and their security clearance may be suspended or downgraded pending an inquiry by the Directorate of Military Intelligence. Officers and JCOs returning from foreign visits are required to submit a detailed visit report within fifteen days of return, covering their interactions, observations, and any unusual contacts or approaches by foreign nationals during the visit. The Ministry of External Affairs and the Intelligence Bureau are kept in the loop for all foreign travel by senior defence personnel of the rank of Major General and above, and any foreign travel by such officers is reported to the Cabinet Secretariat as a matter of routine. Family members accompanying defence personnel on foreign travel are also subject to the provisions of this policy, and their inclusion in the travel plan must be specifically mentioned in the original application for foreign travel clearance.

Deputation of defence personnel to civil departments and Central Government Ministries is governed by the provisions of MoD circular MoD/D(Est)/2015/Deputation/001 and the Department of Personnel and Training guidelines on deputation as applicable to the armed forces, and it provides a mechanism for the mutual exchange of administrative expertise between the military and civilian wings of the Government of India. Officers of the rank of Colonel and above are eligible to be considered for deputation to civil departments, and the deputation is ordinarily for a maximum period of three years, extendable by one year in exceptional circumstances with the approval of the Ministry of Defence. During deputation, the officer's salary, allowances, and benefits continue to be drawn from the defence services estimates, while the civil department to which the officer is deputed reimburses the difference in pay if the post to which the officer is posted carries a higher pay scale, in accordance with the deputation allowance rules. The officer on deputation is governed by the service regulations of the armed forces for matters relating to leave, discipline, conduct, and annual confidential reporting, while the functional responsibilities and work performance are assessed by the civilian reporting authority of the host department and communicated to the Military Secretary's Branch for incorporation in the ACR. The period of deputation counts as service for the purpose of promotion in the armed forces, and an officer on deputation remains within the Zone of Consideration for promotion boards and is considered on the basis of the ACR dossier maintained at the Military Secretary's Branch. An officer on deputation to a civil department may not accept any post or allowance from the host department that is not consistent with the norms for deputation prescribed by the Department of Personnel and Training and the Ministry of Defence, and any violation of this condition is subject to disciplinary proceedings. Reversion from deputation is affected by the Military Secretary's Branch upon receipt of a formal request from the civil department or on the initiative of the Ministry of Defence when the services of the officer are required for a service appointment, and the officer must report back to the Service Headquarters within thirty days of the reversion order. Deputation to certain departments such as the Border Roads Organisation, the Survey of India, and the National Disaster Management Authority is treated differently from deputation to other civil departments, and specific rules apply to such postings as laid down in the respective departmental instructions. Officers are not ordinarily permitted to seek deputation to the same civil department more than once during their service career, and the ceiling on total deputation period during the entire service of an officer is five years. The impact of deputation on military seniority is governed by the rule that an officer who is superseded for promotion during the period of deputation is treated on the same footing as any other officer who is superseded, and reversion from deputation does not automatically entitle an officer to reconsideration by a selection board that has already concluded its proceedings.

The re-employment of retired defence personnel in Central Government departments and Defence Public Sector Undertakings is governed by the provisions of MoD circular No. MoD/D(Res)/2018/ReEmp/017 and the Department of Personnel and Training office memoranda on re-employment of retired government servants, as modified by specific instructions applicable to ex-servicemen. Retired personnel of the rank of Major General and above are subject to a mandatory cooling-off period of one year before they are eligible for re-employment in any post under the Central Government or in any Defence Public Sector Undertaking, and this restriction is intended to prevent the leveraging of recent official influence or access to sensitive information in a post-retirement capacity. For officers of the rank of Colonel and below and for JCOs and other ranks, the cooling-off period is six months from the date of retirement or release from service, subject to the specific requirements of the recruiting organisation. Re-employment is permitted in departments such as the Ordnance Factory Board, the Defence Research and Development Organisation, the Border Roads Organisation, the Indian Coast Guard in advisory roles, the Ministry of Defence in civilian equivalent posts, and in Kendriya Vidyalayas and Sainik Schools where the individual possesses the requisite qualifications. During the period of re-employment, the salary of the retired defence person is regulated such that the pay in the re-employed post plus the pension drawn shall not exceed the last pay drawn at the time of retirement, and if the total exceeds this ceiling, the pay in the re-employed post is reduced proportionately, as provided under the Fundamental Rules applicable to re-employed pensioners. No new pension is earned during the period of re-employment, as the individual continues to draw the pension already sanctioned at the time of retirement, and the period of re-employment does not add to the qualifying service for pension computation purposes. The maximum age limit for re-employment is sixty-two years for officers and sixty years for JCOs and other ranks, and re-employment beyond these ages is not permissible except in the case of highly specialised technical or scientific personnel where the approval of the Ministry of Defence is specifically obtained. The authority to sanction re-employment in Ministry of Defence establishments is the Director General Resettlement for Other Ranks and JCOs, and the Joint Secretary, Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare for officers, and the sanction is issued for a period of one year initially, renewable annually subject to satisfactory performance and continued requirement. All retired defence personnel who seek re-employment are required to submit a declaration confirming that they do not hold any other post under the Government or any public sector undertaking simultaneously, and making a false declaration in this regard is a criminal offence in addition to being grounds for termination of re-employment. The Directorate General Resettlement maintains a database of all re-employed ex-servicemen and publishes an annual report to the Ministry of Defence on the status of re-employment, the departments where re-employed personnel are serving, and any disciplinary cases that may have arisen during the period of re-employment.



The annual defence budget of India is structured into two primary categories of expenditure, namely capital expenditure and revenue expenditure, both of which are presented to the Parliament of India as part of the Union Budget under Demand for Grants Number 20 for the Ministry of Defence, Demand Number 21 for the Department of Military Affairs, Demand Number 22 for the Department of Defence Production, and Demand Number 23 for the Department of Research and Development. Capital expenditure constitutes funds allocated for the acquisition of new assets including weapons platforms, aircraft, naval vessels, armoured fighting vehicles, missiles, satellites, and major defence infrastructure such as airfields, naval dockyards, and fortified border posts. Revenue expenditure covers recurring operational costs including pay and allowances of all service personnel, civilian defence employees, rations, petroleum oil and lubricants, ammunition replenishment, spare parts, routine maintenance, and transportation costs incurred in the normal course of service operations. In the financial year 2024-25, the total defence allocation stood at rupees six lakh twenty-one thousand five hundred and forty crores, representing approximately 1.90 percent of the gross domestic product, with the capital to revenue ratio being approximately 27.67 percent capital and 72.33 percent revenue, a ratio that has been a subject of ongoing deliberation within the Ministry of Defence as a higher capital ratio is considered more conducive to modernisation. The allocation process begins with the Budget Call Letter issued by the Ministry of Finance in September of each year, requiring all Ministries to submit their demands by November, and the Ministry of Defence consolidates the demands of the three services, the Defence Research and Development Organisation, the Ordnance Factories, and the Coast Guard before submitting the consolidated demand to the Department of Expenditure. The budget allocation is approved by the Parliament through the Appropriation Act passed in the Budget Session, and the sanctioned funds are released to the respective service headquarters through the Letter of Credit mechanism operated by the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts, New Delhi in coordination with the Office of the Controller General of Defence Accounts. In cases where the initial allocation proves insufficient due to unforeseen operational requirements, emergency procurements, or exchange rate fluctuations affecting foreign currency payments, the Ministry of Defence may seek additional funds through the Supplementary Demands for Grants, which are presented to the Parliament in two tranches — the first in July and the second in December of each financial year. The flow of funds from the Ministry of Defence to the field formations follows a hierarchical path through the Command Headquarters, the Area Headquarters, and the Sub-Area Headquarters before reaching the unit level, and each level of headquarters is assigned a budget allocation sub-head under which it exercises financial powers as delegated by the financial regulations applicable to the respective service. The Integrated Financial Adviser attached to each Service Headquarters is the principal financial advisor to the Service Chief and plays a critical role in monitoring budget utilisation, providing financial concurrence to procurement proposals, and flagging instances of potential financial irregularity to the Ministry of Defence and the Controller General of Defence Accounts. A quarterly review of budget utilisation is conducted by the Financial Adviser Defence Services in consultation with the respective service headquarters, and corrective action including reappropriation of funds between sub-heads is taken in cases where utilisation is significantly below or above the projected trajectory.

The financial sanctioning powers of defence officers are governed by the Financial Regulations for the Army, 2010 and the corresponding financial regulations for the Navy and Air Force, as periodically updated through Ministry of Defence circulars and Government of India orders. At the lowest level of the hierarchy, a unit Commanding Officer of the rank of Colonel is empowered to sanction expenditure up to rupees twenty-five lakhs per occasion for recurring store purchases, routine maintenance of vehicles and equipment, and minor works of urgency within the unit lines, subject to the funds being available within the unit's sanctioned allocation. The Brigade Commander, of the rank of Brigadier, is empowered to sanction expenditure up to rupees fifty lakhs per occasion for purchases falling within the operational and administrative needs of the brigade group, and all sanctions issued by the Brigade Commander must be concurred in by the Brigade Administrative Officer before the commitment is made. The General Officer Commanding a Division or an Independent Sub-Area, ordinarily of the rank of Major General, is empowered to sanction expenditure up to rupees two crores per occasion for store purchases and up to rupees five crores for works services within the formation area, subject to financial concurrence by the Chief Administrative Officer or the equivalent finance officer of the formation. At the Command level, the General Officer Commanding-in-Chief, of the rank of Lieutenant General, is empowered to sanction expenditure up to rupees twenty-five crores for recurring store and service requirements and up to rupees fifty crores for approved works projects, with the financial concurrence of the Command Financial Adviser being mandatory for all sanctions above rupees ten crores. The Vice Chief of Army Staff and the Chief of Army Staff at the apex of the Army command structure are empowered to sanction individual expenditures of up to rupees fifty crores and one hundred crores respectively, within the overall allocation approved by Parliament, and beyond this limit, the matter must be placed before the Defence Procurement Board or the Defence Acquisition Council as appropriate to the nature and value of the expenditure. The Raksha Mantri is the final sanctioning authority for all capital procurement proposals that exceed the powers of the Chief of Defence Staff and the Service Chiefs, and proposals above rupees five hundred crores are required to be placed before the Cabinet Committee on Security for approval, as mandated under the extant Defence Procurement Procedure. The Chief of Defence Staff, a post created in December 2019 and currently held with the rank of General, has been delegated financial powers commensurate with the role of the Department of Military Affairs, and is empowered to sanction joint services expenditure for common use equipment and services up to rupees one hundred crores per occasion with the concurrence of the Joint Financial Adviser, Department of Military Affairs. At the level of JCOs, the Subedar Major or equivalent senior JCO does not independently exercise financial sanctioning powers but may countersign petty cash vouchers up to rupees five hundred in canteen and mess accounts under the supervision of the unit Quartermaster. All sanctions issued under delegated financial powers must be recorded in the sanctions register maintained at the level of the sanctioning authority, and a quarterly return of all sanctions issued must be submitted to the next higher financial authority for review and record.

The capital procurement budget of the Ministry of Defence is the most closely scrutinised component of the defence budget, reflecting the Government's commitment to the modernisation of the armed forces and the degree to which indigenous defence production is being encouraged through the Make in India initiative. In the financial year 2024-25, the capital outlay for defence was allocated at rupees one lakh seventy-two thousand crores, of which approximately forty-eight percent was earmarked for committed liabilities arising from contracts concluded in previous financial years, including the Rafale aircraft contract, the P-8I maritime patrol aircraft replenishment contract, the S-400 Triumf air defence system contract, and various naval vessel construction contracts awarded to public sector shipyards. The allocation for new schemes, which refers to fresh capital commitments for equipment and infrastructure not yet under contract, is determined after netting out the committed liabilities from the total capital budget, and the residual amount is distributed among the three services by the Defence Acquisition Council in accordance with the priority list of capital acquisitions maintained at the Service Headquarters. Long-term procurement planning is formalized through the Integrated Capability Development Plan, formerly known as the Long-Term Integrated Perspective Plan, which is a fifteen-year rolling plan prepared jointly by the three services and the Department of Military Affairs, and which forms the basis for phased budgetary provisioning for future acquisitions. Committed liabilities, which are annual instalments payable against multi-year contracts already signed, receive first priority in budget allocation, and the Finance Ministry is obligated under the terms of such contracts to ensure that the instalments are funded without interruption, as failure to honour payment schedules may attract penalty clauses and damage India's standing as a defence procurement partner. The Defence Procurement Procedure 2020, also referred to as DPP-2020 and subsequently supplemented by the Defence Acquisition Procedure 2020, lays down the framework within which capital procurement proposals progress from the Acceptance of Necessity stage to the signing of the contract, and financial implications at each stage are formally cleared by the Integrated Financial Adviser at the service headquarters level and by the Financial Adviser, Ministry of Defence at the Ministry level. For joint procurement by two or more services of common equipment such as transport aircraft, mid-air refuelling platforms, communication satellites, and battlefield management systems, the capital budget allocation is made to a single nodal service designated by the Defence Acquisition Council, which then manages the procurement on behalf of all user services. The transfer of capital funds from one service to another within the same financial year is permissible only with the approval of the Ministry of Defence and the concurrence of the Ministry of Finance, and such transfers are formally recorded as reappropriations in the government accounts. Exchange rate fluctuations significantly impact the capital procurement budget in respect of foreign vendor contracts denominated in US Dollars, Euros, Russian Roubles, or other foreign currencies, and the Ministry of Defence maintains a provision for exchange rate variation in the capital budget estimates, although in years of significant depreciation of the Indian Rupee this provision has frequently proven inadequate. The Controller General of Defence Accounts oversees the financial accounting of all capital expenditure through the network of Principal Controllers and Controllers of Defence Accounts stationed at each Command headquarters, and the annual capital expenditure statement is incorporated in the Report of the Comptroller and Auditor General of India tabled before the Parliament.

Revenue expenditure management in the defence services is a complex administrative exercise involving the simultaneous management of multiple sub-heads across thousands of units and establishments spread across the length and breadth of the country and in operational areas along the borders. The primary components of revenue expenditure are pay and allowances of approximately fourteen lakh active service personnel and nearly four lakh civilian defence employees, the rations supply to all troops under the Composite Ration Scale, the procurement of petroleum oil and lubricants for all service vehicles and equipment, the maintenance of weapons and equipment, the provision of ammunition for training, exercises, and operational requirements, and the expenditure on communication, information technology, and medical services. The salaries and allowances component of revenue expenditure is non-discretionary and is drawn monthly by all units through the Pay Accounts Offices managed under the Controller General of Defence Accounts, and any delay or shortfall in this component would directly affect the welfare of serving personnel and would constitute a serious administrative failure. The allocation for petroleum oil and lubricants, which includes aviation turbine fuel for the Air Force and Navy aviation assets, high-speed diesel for all ground vehicles, marine fuel for naval vessels, and lubricants for all mechanical equipment, is one of the most volatile components of the revenue budget, being directly influenced by international crude oil prices and the exchange rate of the Indian Rupee against the US Dollar. The Quartermaster General's Branch at Army Headquarters and the equivalent branches at Naval and Air Headquarters maintain a monthly expenditure plan against the annual petroleum allocation, and any variation in fuel prices above the budget estimate is brought to the notice of the Ministry of Defence for seeking additional funds through the Supplementary Demands for Grants. Ration supply to all troops is managed through the Army Service Corps, and the Composite Ration Scale determines the daily calorific entitlement of personnel at peace stations, field stations, and operational areas, with the field scale being approximately thirty-five percent more calorific than the peace scale to account for the additional physical exertion of operational duty. Ammunition expenditure for training is regulated through the Annual Ammunition Training Scales laid down by the Directorate General of Artillery and the equivalent directorates of the Armoured Corps, Infantry, and Aviation, and the demand for training ammunition must be projected at least twelve months in advance so that the Ordnance Factory Board can plan production accordingly. Monthly budget utilisation reports are prepared at the unit level by the Regimental Officer or the equivalent administrative officer, consolidated at the formation level, and submitted to the respective Command Headquarters by the fifteenth of each subsequent month, and the Command headquarters submits a consolidated utilisation report to the Service Headquarters by the twenty-fifth of the month. Reappropriation of funds between revenue sub-heads within the same formation is permitted within the financial powers of the respective sanctioning authority without reference to a higher authority, provided the reappropriation does not alter the total revenue allocation of the formation and is not applied to a sub-head that is specifically earmarked or ring-fenced by the Ministry of Defence. The Financial Adviser attached to each Command Headquarters conducts a mid-year review in October of each financial year, at which point the projected year-end utilisation is compared against the annual allocation, and proposals for surrender of savings or for additional demands are initiated based on the projected outturn.

Emergency procurement financial rules applicable to the defence services are governed by Rule 164 of the General Financial Rules 2017 as adapted for defence procurement under the Defence Procurement Procedure 2020, and the provisions of the relevant paragraphs of the Financial Regulations for the Army, supplemented by Ministry of Defence circular No. MoD/D(Fin)/Emergency/2019/0456 dated March 22, 2019. Emergency procurement is permissible when the normal procurement process, which involves publication of tender enquiry, evaluation of bids, negotiation, and approval through the prescribed competency levels, cannot be completed within the time available due to an operational emergency, a natural disaster requiring immediate military support, or a critical serviceability failure in an essential weapons system or platform that poses a direct risk to operational readiness. The financial limit for emergency procurement at the unit level, sanctioned by the Commanding Officer without competitive tendering, is rupees five lakhs per occasion, and such procurement must be from an empanelled vendor or a Government-authorised source wherever possible. At the formation level of a Division or equivalent, the emergency procurement limit is rupees fifty lakhs per occasion, sanctioned by the General Officer Commanding with the financial concurrence of the Chief Administrative Officer. At the Command level, the General Officer Commanding-in-Chief is empowered to sanction emergency procurement of up to rupees two crores per occasion, and at the level of the Vice Chief of Army Staff, the limit extends to rupees ten crores, beyond which the matter must be placed before the Raksha Mantri on an urgent basis with a certificate of operational necessity issued by the Chief of the respective service. Post-procurement documentation requirements are stringent and must be completed within ninety days of the emergency purchase, and include a formal explanation of the nature of the emergency, the steps taken to ascertain the reasonableness of the price, a confirmation that the item was not available through the normal supply chain, and a post-facto audit by the station finance officer. The Comptroller and Auditor General of India pays particular attention to emergency procurement cases during its annual audit of defence accounts, and cases where the emergency nature of the procurement is not clearly established through documentary evidence are subject to audit objection and financial recovery from the sanctioning authority. A statement of all emergency procurements exceeding rupees one lakh in value must be compiled quarterly at the Command level and submitted to the Service Headquarters for review by the Integrated Financial Adviser, who is required to certify that the emergency provisions have not been misused as a device to avoid normal competitive procurement procedures. The misuse of emergency procurement powers to obtain goods or services from preferred vendors without competitive tendering, or the fabrication of an emergency to justify procurement that was foreseeable and could have been processed through normal channels, is a financial irregularity that may result in disciplinary action against the sanctioning officer and recovery of any excess expenditure determined by the audit authority. The Ministry of Defence reviews the patterns of emergency procurement annually as part of the budget review process and uses such data to recommend adjustments to standard provisioning scales, so as to reduce reliance on emergency procurement in future years.

The Make in India initiative as applied to defence procurement is governed by the Defence Acquisition Procedure 2020, specifically Chapter II, which introduces the categorisation of procurement into six categories in order of priority, with the Indigenously Designed, Developed, and Manufactured category at the top, followed by Buy Indian, Buy and Make Indian, Buy and Make, Buy Global with Transfer of Technology, and finally Buy Global at the bottom of the preference hierarchy. For the Indigenously Designed, Developed, and Manufactured category, the minimum indigenous content threshold is fifty percent of the total contract value, and for the Buy Indian category the minimum indigenous content is sixty percent, whereas for the Buy and Make Indian category the indigenous content must reach fifty percent by the end of the production phase, rising from an initial threshold of thirty percent at the commencement of production. Foreign vendors who are awarded contracts under categories that permit foreign participation are subject to mandatory offset obligations under which they are required to invest a minimum of thirty percent of the contract value in the Indian defence industry through the acquisition of goods or services from Indian defence manufacturers, investment in Indian defence joint ventures, or transfer of technology to Indian entities, as certified by the offset implementation agency designated by the Ministry of Defence. Penalties for non-fulfilment of offset obligations include financial penalties calculated at five percent of the unmet offset value per year, and persistent non-compliance may result in blacklisting of the vendor from future defence procurement in India, and the financial impact of such penalties is recorded in the accounts of the Ministry of Defence as non-tax revenue receipts. The Make in India policy has a direct impact on budget planning in that equipment manufactured domestically by the Ordnance Factory Board, Hindustan Aeronautics Limited, Mazagon Dock Shipbuilders Limited, and other Defence Public Sector Undertakings is procured in Indian Rupees, thereby eliminating the exchange rate risk that affects foreign currency procurement, and the Ministry of Finance has acknowledged this as a positive fiscal benefit of the policy. For procurement from private Indian defence manufacturers who are empanelled under the Indian Vendor Registration Scheme maintained by the Defence Production Board, the financial evaluation criteria include not only the quoted price but also the vendor's capability to sustain production, maintain quality, and provide after-sales support, and vendors failing any of these criteria may be disqualified despite quoting the lowest price. The Defence Production Policy 2020 sets a target of achieving a domestic defence production turnover of rupees one lakh seventy-five thousand crores by the financial year 2024-25, with exports constituting rupees thirty-five thousand crores of this target, and the budgetary implications of supporting this target include provisioning for industrial infrastructure grants, research and development subsidies under the Technology Development Fund, and concessional financing through the Defence Investor Cell. Separate provisions in the capital budget are made for Make procedures, which are procurement cases where the Ministry of Defence funds a portion of the development cost incurred by Indian manufacturers, and under Make-I the Government funds up to seventy percent of the development cost, while under Make-II the entire development cost is borne by the industry with recoupment through the subsequent production contract. The Defence Acquisition Council, chaired by the Raksha Mantri, reviews the implementation of Make in India targets on a half-yearly basis and directs corrective action where the indigenous content or offset compliance is below the stipulated thresholds, and the financial data underlying this review is consolidated by the Department of Defence Production and the Department of Military Affairs jointly. Budget allocations for programmes under the Make in India framework are ring-fenced within the capital budget and cannot be reappropriated to meet committed liabilities from foreign procurement contracts without the specific approval of the Defence Acquisition Council and the Ministry of Finance.

Foreign vendor procurement under the defence budget involves a set of financial instruments and contractual safeguards that are distinct from domestic procurement and are governed by Chapter VI of the Defence Acquisition Procedure 2020, the Foreign Exchange Management Act 1999, and the Reserve Bank of India guidelines on advance payments to foreign entities. The primary challenge of foreign vendor procurement from a financial management perspective is the exposure to exchange rate fluctuation, which is managed through a combination of budget estimates based on a hedged exchange rate determined jointly by the Ministry of Finance and the Reserve Bank of India at the commencement of the financial year, and a provision for exchange rate variation that is maintained as a lump sum in the capital budget. Payment terms in Government-to-Government defence contracts, such as those concluded between India and Russia, the United States, France, and Israel, are typically structured with an advance payment not exceeding fifteen percent of the total contract value upon signing, progress payments linked to identified production milestones, and a final payment upon delivery and acceptance of the equipment by the Indian Armed Forces. Performance Bank Guarantees are mandatory for all foreign vendor contracts above rupees fifty crores, and the Performance Bank Guarantee must be provided by a bank of international standing or by a bank acceptable to the Reserve Bank of India, and the guarantee must remain valid for the entire warranty period of the equipment plus six months. In cases where the foreign vendor fails to deliver within the contractually stipulated timeline, Liquidated Damages are invoked at a rate of half percent of the contract value per week of delay, subject to a maximum of ten percent of the total contract value, and the recovery of liquidated damages is effected through the encashment of the Performance Bank Guarantee or by deduction from payments due to the vendor. For Government-to-Government deals, the financial structure involves inter-governmental payment arrangements that may include rupee-rouble payments, special Vostro account mechanisms, and credit lines extended by the vendor country's Export Credit Agency, and the Ministry of Finance is closely involved in the structuring of such arrangements to ensure compliance with India's balance of payments obligations. The advance payment to a foreign vendor for capital procurement is routed through the Reserve Bank of India after clearance by the Ministry of Finance, and the advance is released only after the vendor provides an Advance Payment Bank Guarantee of equivalent value from an acceptable bank, ensuring that the Indian Government can recover the advance in the event of non-performance. Foreign exchange requirements for defence payments are projected by the Ministry of Defence at the commencement of each financial year and are communicated to the Reserve Bank of India through the Ministry of Finance, and the Reserve Bank of India factors these requirements into its foreign exchange reserve management strategy. The annual outgo in foreign exchange for defence procurement has in recent years ranged from eight billion US Dollars to twelve billion US Dollars, and a significant portion of this is on account of energy imports for the Indian Navy's nuclear-powered submarines, which falls under a separate classified budget head not publicly disclosed in the Union Budget documents. The Ministry of Defence maintains a Register of Foreign Exchange Commitments at the level of the Joint Secretary responsible for capital acquisition, and this register is reconciled monthly against the statements provided by the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts and the Reserve Bank of India to ensure accuracy of the outstanding foreign exchange liability.

Cross-department procurement coordination among the three services of the Indian Armed Forces is a financial efficiency imperative recognised by the Ministry of Defence as essential to avoiding duplication of expenditure on common-use equipment and to achieving economies of scale in procurement. The Department of Military Affairs, under the Chief of Defence Staff, has been specifically mandated with the responsibility of identifying equipment common to two or more services, consolidating the demand of the services into a single integrated procurement case, and managing the procurement on behalf of all user services, thereby avoiding the situation where each service independently procures the same or similar equipment at different prices and under different contract conditions. Communication systems are a primary area of joint procurement coordination, and the procurement of software-defined radios for use by the Army, the Air Force, and the Indian Navy has been structured as a single consolidated tender with a combined quantity that has resulted in a price reduction of approximately eighteen percent compared to the prices obtained in earlier service-specific individual procurements. Radar systems for airfield approach control, maritime surveillance, and artillery fire control have been identified as further areas for joint procurement, and the Indian Electronics and Radar Development Establishment under the Defence Research and Development Organisation has been tasked with developing common radar platforms that can be adapted for use by all three services, thereby reducing the development cost that would otherwise be separately incurred for each service. Transport vehicles, including medium-duty trucks, high-mobility vehicles, and buses for garrison transport, are procured through the consolidated Truck and Vehicle Procurement Programme managed by the Director General of Ordnance Services at Army Headquarters on behalf of all three services, and the annual contract for standard vehicles is placed with approved vendors following a comprehensive tendering process coordinated by the Directorate. The financial benefit of joint procurement is calculated annually by the Department of Military Affairs and is reported in the Annual Report of the Ministry of Defence, and in the financial year 2023-24, the estimated savings from joint procurement programmes across all categories were placed at rupees three thousand two hundred crores when compared to the cost of independent service-specific procurement at individually negotiated prices. Joint procurement programmes require the establishment of an integrated project management structure in which each service nominates a technical and financial representative to the joint procurement committee, and all decisions on technical specifications, vendor selection, and contract terms are taken by the committee on a consensus basis, with the Department of Military Affairs representative serving as the convening authority. The financial allocation for joint procurement is made initially to the nodal service designated by the Department of Military Affairs, and upon completion of procurement, the cost is apportioned among the user services in proportion to the quantities procured on behalf of each service, and the apportionment is effected through inter-service fund transfers approved by the Controller General of Defence Accounts. Disagreements between services on technical specifications are the most common source of delay in joint procurement, and the Department of Military Affairs has issued directions that in cases of irreconcilable technical divergence, the requirement of the service with the largest quantity shall be adopted as the baseline specification, with adaptations for other services funded from their respective capital allocations. The annual joint procurement plan is prepared by the Department of Military Affairs in January of each year for the following financial year, and it forms the basis for the allocation of capital budget funds to joint procurement programmes in the Union Budget presented in February.

Advance payment to vendors in defence procurement is governed by Rule 257 of the General Financial Rules 2017 as adapted for defence procurement and by Paragraph 48 of the Defence Procurement Procedure 2020, which sets out the conditions under which advance payment is permissible and the safeguards required to protect the financial interest of the Government of India. Advance payment to domestic vendors is ordinarily not permissible under the financial regulations, and the standard payment term for domestic procurement is payment within thirty days of delivery and acceptance of stores, which is the practice followed for all Ordnance Factory Board supplies, Defence Public Sector Undertaking supplies, and private Indian vendor supplies under standard contracts. An exception to this rule is made for long-lead procurement, which refers to items that require a prolonged manufacturing cycle and where the vendor must incur significant upfront expenditure on raw materials and tooling before production can commence, and in such cases an advance payment of up to fifteen percent of the contract value may be sanctioned with the approval of the competent financial authority. For foreign vendor contracts, advance payment of up to fifteen percent of the contract value is standard practice and is explicitly provided for in the Defence Acquisition Procedure 2020, with the mandatory requirement that the vendor provides an Advance Payment Bank Guarantee for the full amount of the advance, valid until the advance is recovered through deduction from milestone payments. The Advance Payment Bank Guarantee must be issued by a scheduled commercial bank in India or by an international bank of standing approved by the Reserve Bank of India, and the guarantee must contain an unconditional and irrevocable clause allowing the Government of India to invoke the guarantee without prior notice to the vendor in case of non-performance. In cases where advance payment has been made and the vendor subsequently fails to deliver the contracted stores or equipment, the recovery procedure is initiated by the Contracts Directorate of the respective Service Headquarters, and the Performance Bank Guarantee is simultaneously invoked to ensure that both the advance and the liquidated damages are recoverable. The maximum advance permissible for procurement of software and technical services is different from that for hardware, and the financial regulations permit advance payment of up to twenty-five percent for software development contracts on the ground that software development requires sustained intellectual resources without corresponding material delivery milestones in the early phases. All advance payments made to domestic or foreign vendors must be reported in the monthly expenditure statement of the formation or headquarters concerned, and a separate register of advances paid and recovered must be maintained at the level of the Contracts Directorate for scrutiny by the audit authorities. The Integrated Financial Adviser at Service Headquarters level reviews the register of outstanding advances on a quarterly basis and initiates action where advances remain unrecovered beyond the contractually stipulated recovery schedule, and persistent non-recovery is reported to the Ministry of Defence as a potential financial risk. Advance payment is not permissible for procurement through the Ordnance Depots or the Army Base Workshops except in specific cases certified by the Army Headquarters as essential to maintain operational inventory levels, and even in such cases the advance must be sanctioned by an authority not below the rank of Major General with the concurrence of the Command Financial Adviser.

The procedure for writing off obsolete and unserviceable equipment in the defence services is governed by the provisions of Regulations for the Army, specifically Regulation 504 and the associated appendices, and the corresponding regulations of the Navy and Air Force, together with Ministry of Defence circular No. MoD/D(Fin)/WriteOff/2020/0034 dated January 15, 2020, which consolidated and updated the previous write-off rules in line with the revised financial delegation notified under the Seventh Central Pay Commission recommendations. A Board of Officers is required to be convened by the Commanding Officer or formation commander as appropriate to examine the condition of equipment proposed for write-off, and the Board must comprise at least three officers including one who is technically qualified in the equipment being examined, and the Board's proceedings must record the original cost of the equipment, the current book value, the reason for unserviceability or obsolescence, the possibility of repair or refurbishment, and the recommended method of disposal. The approval authority for write-off of equipment varies by the value of the equipment as recorded in the government accounts, and a write-off up to rupees one lakh may be sanctioned by the Commanding Officer, up to rupees five lakhs by the Brigade Commander, up to rupees twenty-five lakhs by the General Officer Commanding a Division, up to rupees one crore by the General Officer Commanding-in-Chief at the Command level, up to rupees five crores by the Vice Chief of Army Staff, and beyond rupees five crores the sanction of the Raksha Mantri is required. Equipment identified for write-off as scrap is required to be sold to the Metal Scrap Trading Corporation of India, which is the authorised agency for disposal of Government scrap, or to other agencies specifically authorised by the Ministry of Defence, and the sale must be conducted through a competitive auction process to maximise the sale proceeds, which are deposited as non-tax revenue receipts into the Consolidated Fund of India. The financial accounting of write-offs requires that the book value of the written-off equipment be deleted from the unit ledger account maintained by the unit Quartermaster and that the deletion be endorsed by the Regimental Officer and verified by the audit officer of the station. Weapons systems, aircraft, and naval vessels that are written off as unserviceable or obsolete are subject to a separate demilitarisation procedure in addition to the financial write-off, and the demilitarisation must be certified by a technically qualified board of officers before the hulk or remnants are offered to the Metal Scrap Trading Corporation or other authorised agencies. The sale of salvaged parts from written-off equipment to private parties is prohibited except with the specific approval of the Ministry of Defence, and any such sale must be conducted through a transparent public auction with a reserve price determined by the board of officers. The Comptroller and Auditor General of India scrutinises write-off cases during its annual audit and frequently raises objections to cases where the write-off has been sanctioned without a properly constituted Board of Officers, where the sale of scrap has not been conducted competitively, or where there is evidence that equipment was allowed to deteriorate to an unserviceable condition through neglect rather than normal wear. Write-off of equipment that is lost, stolen, or destroyed in a fire or flood requires a separate inquiry under Rule 177 of the Army Rules 1954 to establish the cause of the loss and the personal liability if any of the individual responsible for the custody of the equipment, and the findings of the inquiry must accompany the write-off proposal submitted to the sanctioning authority. Write-off of stores and equipment does not in itself absolve the individual responsible for the loss from personal liability, and where negligence or misconduct is established, financial recovery may be ordered independently of the write-off sanction, with recovery being effected from the pay and allowances of the individual concerned over a period not exceeding twelve monthly instalments.

The infrastructure budget for defence establishments encompasses the construction of barracks, officers' messes, married quarters, hospitals, ammunition depots, airfields, naval dockyards, forward operating bases, and border infrastructure roads, and is managed under the Capital Works sub-head of the Defence budget, which in the financial year 2024-25 was allocated rupees twelve thousand five hundred crores for the Army and Air Force works services and a separate allocation of rupees eight thousand crores for naval dockyard infrastructure under the Department of Military Affairs. All construction works for the Army and Air Force are planned and executed by the Military Engineering Services, which is a joint service organisation under the Engineer-in-Chief at Army Headquarters, and for the Navy the construction of dockyard infrastructure is managed by the Naval Works Department under the Flag Officer Commanding-in-Chief of the respective Naval Command. The approval procedure for infrastructure projects is structured in a tiered manner depending on the estimated cost of the project, and works costing up to rupees two crores are sanctioned by the General Officer Commanding of the Division or Air Officer Commanding of the Air Force Station, while works between rupees two crores and ten crores require the sanction of the Command headquarters, and works above ten crores require the approval of the Ministry of Defence with the concurrence of the Integrated Financial Adviser at Service Headquarters. Major infrastructure projects such as the construction of a new fighter aircraft base, a new naval dock, or a new ammunition depot are treated as Capital Works Projects and require preparation of a detailed project report including architectural drawings, soil investigation reports, environmental clearances, and financial analysis, which must be vetted by the Military Engineering Services headquarters before being submitted to the Ministry of Defence for sanction. Border Roads Organisation, which functions under the Department of Military Affairs and is commanded by a Director General of the rank of Lieutenant General, is responsible for the construction and maintenance of roads in border areas, and its annual budget allocation in the financial year 2024-25 was rupees six thousand five hundred crores, which covers both new construction and the maintenance of approximately sixty thousand kilometres of strategic roads in difficult terrain. Cost escalation in defence infrastructure projects is a recurring financial management challenge, and the rules permit cost escalation up to ten percent of the originally sanctioned cost to be approved by the sanctioning authority itself, while escalation between ten and twenty-five percent requires the approval of the next higher authority, and escalation exceeding twenty-five percent of the original cost requires a fresh appraisal and sanction as if it were a new project. The Military Engineering Services charges an agency fee of a specified percentage of the project cost, which is included in the overall project cost estimate, and this agency fee covers the cost of design, supervision, and quality control services provided by the Military Engineering Services throughout the project. Land acquisition for new defence infrastructure is processed through the Ministry of Defence in coordination with the Ministry of Housing and Urban Affairs, the Department of Land Resources, and the concerned State Government, and the cost of land acquisition under the Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013 has significantly increased the upfront cost of new infrastructure projects in recent years. The Cantonment Boards, which provide civic services in defence cantonments under the Cantonment Act 2006, have a separate budget funded through a combination of cantonment fund revenues and grants from the Ministry of Defence, and the capital budget of the Cantonment Board for major repairs and new amenity construction is included in the overall infrastructure budget of the Ministry of Defence. Environmental clearances from the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change are mandatory for all defence infrastructure projects in ecologically sensitive zones, and the cost and time involved in obtaining such clearances must be factored into the project schedule and financial estimates submitted to the Ministry of Defence for sanction.

The budget allocation for the Defence Research and Development Organisation is made under Demand for Grants Number 23 under the head of Department of Defence Research and Development, and in the financial year 2024-25 the total allocation to the Defence Research and Development Organisation was rupees twenty-three thousand eight hundred and fifty-five crores, representing approximately 5.25 percent of the total defence budget. The Defence Research and Development Organisation comprises fifty-two laboratories and establishments spread across India, covering domains including aerospace, armaments, combat vehicles, electronics and communication, life sciences, materials, missiles, naval systems, and advanced numerical and materials research, and the budget allocation to each laboratory is made on the basis of the laboratory's approved programme of work for the financial year. The basis for budget allocation is the Mission Mode Project funding framework, under which each major development programme is funded as a standalone project with a total project cost approved by the Defence Research and Development Board and released in tranches upon achievement of defined technical milestones, and this milestone-based fund release mechanism is intended to prevent cost overruns by ensuring that funding for subsequent phases is contingent on the demonstrated success of earlier phases. The Technology Development Fund, managed by the Defence Research and Development Organisation, provides financial assistance to Indian private sector companies and academic institutions for the development of technologies needed by the armed forces, and the total corpus of the Technology Development Fund in the financial year 2024-25 was rupees five hundred crores, with individual grants ranging from rupees ten crores to rupees fifty crores depending on the complexity of the technology and the track record of the grantee. The balance between indigenous research and development and foreign import is a perennial challenge for the defence budget planners, and the Ministry of Defence has directed that for equipment categories where the Defence Research and Development Organisation has declared a technology readiness level of seven or above, which indicates that the technology is ready for prototype development, preference must be given to domestic development over import, even if the imported option may be available at a lower cost in the short term. The Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme, the Light Combat Aircraft programme, the Arjun Main Battle Tank, the Advanced Towed Artillery Gun System, and the Pinaka Multi-Barrel Rocket System are examples of major Defence Research and Development Organisation programmes that have been funded over multiple decades and across multiple financial years, and the total expenditure on these programmes has in each case significantly exceeded the original cost estimates at the time of project sanction, underscoring the challenge of financial management in long-duration indigenous development programmes. The Comptroller and Auditor General of India has, in multiple reports, drawn attention to time and cost overruns in Defence Research and Development Organisation projects and has recommended the introduction of more rigorous milestone-based fund release mechanisms and independent technical audits of project progress before each tranche of funding is released. The Defence Research and Development Organisation budget also includes a provision for the procurement of foreign technology licences, royalties for use of patents, and membership subscriptions to international scientific organisations, all of which are non-negotiable financial commitments that must be protected even when the overall budget is under stress. The Raksha Mantri's Committee on Defence Research, which meets quarterly, reviews the progress of all major Defence Research and Development Organisation projects against the approved milestones and the financial expenditure against the approved budget, and directs remedial action in cases of significant deviation. The annual report of the Defence Research and Development Organisation, tabled in the Parliament along with the demands for grants, discloses the financial progress of each major project against the total project cost and the milestones achieved in the financial year under review, and this report forms the basis for Parliamentary scrutiny through the Standing Committee on Defence.

The defence pension budget is the most rapidly growing component of the Ministry of Defence's expenditure, and it is presented as a separate demand for grants under Demand Number 25, which in the financial year 2024-25 was allocated rupees one lakh forty-one thousand crores, representing approximately 23.5 percent of the total defence budget and an increase of approximately eight percent over the previous year's allocation. The defence pension budget covers the pension and family pension of approximately thirty-three lakh retired defence personnel and their dependants, the disability pension payable to personnel invalided out of service on account of service attributable disabilities, the war injury pension, the gallantry award annuities, the liberalised pension of battle casualties, and the Ex-Servicemen Contributory Health Scheme contributions. The One Rank One Pension scheme, which was formally implemented by the Government of India through a notification dated November 7, 2015 and subsequently amended following judicial proceedings, has had a significant and continuing financial impact on the defence pension budget, as it required the revision of pensions of all pre-2015 retirees to bring them in line with the pension payable to personnel who retired in 2013 in the same rank and with the same length of service, and the arrears payment and subsequent annual revision cycles have added a significant recurring financial burden. The One Rank One Pension revision is undertaken every five years, and the financial provisioning for the upcoming revision, which was due in 2019 and has been subject to judicial scrutiny, has been a major source of uncertainty in the defence pension budget estimates, with the Ministry of Defence maintaining a contingency provision of approximately rupees eight thousand crores in each financial year since 2019 to cover potential arrears payable upon finalisation of the revision. The Ex-Servicemen Contributory Health Scheme is funded through a combination of contributions from retired personnel and a grant from the Ministry of Defence, and in the financial year 2024-25 the Ministry of Defence's contribution to the Ex-Servicemen Contributory Health Scheme was rupees five thousand three hundred crores, which covers the cost of treatment in empanelled hospitals, the maintenance of ECHS polyclinics across the country, and the purchase of medicines under the ECHS drug formulary. The growing defence pension budget as a proportion of the total defence budget is a concern that has been flagged by successive Finance Commissions and by the Comptroller and Auditor General of India, as it reduces the proportion of the defence budget available for operational requirements and capital modernisation, and the Ministry of Defence has initiated a study on the feasibility of introducing a funded pension model for future entrants into the defence services. The calculation of pension entitlement for each retired person is managed by the Office of the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts, which is the pension disbursing authority for the majority of defence retirees, and the calculation is based on the last pay drawn, the number of years of qualifying service, and the applicable pension formula as modified by successive Pay Commission recommendations. Family pension for the widow or widower of a deceased defence person is calculated at thirty percent of the last pay drawn in the case of normal family pension and fifty percent in the case of enhanced family pension applicable to personnel who died attributable to service, and the enhanced family pension is payable for a period of seven years or until the widow or widower remarries or attains the age of sixty-seven, whichever is earlier. Gallantry award annuities, which are paid in addition to normal pension to holders of the Param Vir Chakra, Maha Vir Chakra, Vir Chakra, and other gallantry decorations, are a protected financial commitment that is never adjusted or reduced irrespective of changes to the pension formula, and the annuity amounts were substantially enhanced in the financial year 2017-18 following a review ordered by the Ministry of Defence. The Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare, in coordination with the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts, maintains a comprehensive database of all pension beneficiaries, and the Jeevan Pramaan digital life certificate initiative has been implemented to enable retired personnel to submit their annual life certificate digitally without being required to present themselves physically at a designated bank branch or government office.

The audit of defence expenditure by the Comptroller and Auditor General of India is conducted under the provisions of the Comptroller and Auditor General's Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service Act 1971, and the Defence Audit Wing of the Indian Audit and Accounts Department is specifically responsible for auditing the accounts of the Ministry of Defence, the three armed services, the Defence Public Sector Undertakings, and the Ordnance Factories. Audit objections arising from the audit of defence accounts are communicated to the Ministry of Defence and the concerned service headquarters through Inspection Reports and Draft Paragraphs, and the concerned authority is required to submit a formal reply within a period of six weeks from the date of receipt of the inspection report, setting out the factual position, the reasons for the objection, and the remedial action taken or proposed. In cases where the audit authority concludes that expenditure has been incurred in an irregular, unauthorised, or wasteful manner, a formal Audit Para is issued and is included in the Report of the Comptroller and Auditor General of India on the Defence Services, which is laid before the Parliament and examined by the Public Accounts Committee. The Public Accounts Committee, which is a Parliamentary Standing Committee, calls the Secretary, Ministry of Defence and the Financial Adviser Defence Services to appear before it to explain the facts and circumstances underlying each audit para, and the Ministry is required to submit an Action Taken Report within six months of the Public Accounts Committee's recommendations being tabled in the Parliament. Financial recovery in cases of disallowed expenditure is effected through the standard government recovery mechanism, and the recovery order is issued by the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts on the basis of the audit finding and the concurrence of the Ministry of Defence, and the recovery is made from the pay and allowances of the officer or official responsible for the irregular expenditure in monthly instalments not exceeding one-third of the individual's net monthly emoluments. Officers against whom audit objections are pending, particularly those involving personal financial liability, are required to disclose this fact at the time of annual confidential report initiation, and a pending audit objection involving personal liability may adversely affect the officer's assessment at a promotion selection board. The Directorate of Audit, Defence Services, which operates under the Comptroller and Auditor General and is headed by a Director General of Audit of the Indian Audit and Accounts Service, conducts both regularity audit to check compliance with financial rules and performance audit to assess whether Government expenditure has achieved the intended outcomes in terms of operational capability and value for money. Common categories of audit objections in defence accounts include excess payment of pay and allowances due to incorrect application of pay rules, overpayment to contractors due to measurement irregularities in works contracts, advances paid without proper bank guarantees, procurement at prices higher than approved rates without adequate justification, and failure to recover liquidated damages from defaulting vendors. The Controller General of Defence Accounts issues an internal audit inspection report simultaneously with the Comptroller and Auditor General's inspection report in cases of significant irregularity, and the internal audit team's recommendations are required to be implemented by the concerned unit or headquarters within ninety days of receipt. The Ministry of Defence submits a Consolidated Statement of Audit Objections Pending at the end of each financial year to the Ministry of Finance as part of the compliance reporting framework under the Government Accounts Rules 1990, and persistent non-settlement of audit objections beyond three years may result in the Ministry of Finance imposing a proportionate reduction in the following year's budget allocation as a fiscal discipline measure.

The phenomenon of year-end expenditure acceleration in the defence budget, commonly referred to as the March rush, is a well-documented administrative challenge arising from the provision of the Consolidated Fund of India rules that unspent budget allocations lapse at the end of the financial year on March 31 and cannot be carried forward to the next financial year. Defence formations and units, having conserved budget allocations in the earlier quarters of the financial year due to administrative delays in procurement, slow progress of works contracts, or cautious spending pending finalisation of policy decisions, find themselves in a position in February and March of needing to commit or expend funds rapidly to prevent lapsing of the allocation, and this urgency frequently leads to compromised procurement processes, acceptance of higher prices, and inadequate verification of delivery before payment. The Ministry of Finance issued Office Memorandum No. 1(8)/IC/2022 dated January 18, 2022, directing all Ministries including the Ministry of Defence to restrict expenditure in the last quarter of the financial year to thirty-three percent of the annual allocation and to ensure that no bulk procurement, no major contract signing, and no advance payment to vendors is resorted to in March solely for the purpose of utilising budget allocations that would otherwise lapse. The Ministry of Defence has issued corresponding instructions to all service headquarters and command headquarters reinforcing the Ministry of Finance directive and directing the Integrated Financial Adviser at each level to refuse financial concurrence to any proposal for large-scale procurement initiated after the fifteenth of February without a clear demonstration of operational justification pre-dating the current financial year. The surrender of savings is the complementary procedure through which a formation or unit that identifies in the course of the year that a portion of its allocation is unlikely to be spent communicates this to the higher financial authority by a specified date, so that the Ministry of Defence can apply the savings to other pressing requirements, seek a downward revision of the allocation in the Supplementary Demands for Grants, or surrender the funds to the Ministry of Finance. The last date for surrender of savings under the budget control calendar issued by the Ministry of Finance is typically January 31 for the current financial year, and any savings identified after this date cannot formally be redeployed and result in the lapsing of funds at year end. Lapsing of funds in the capital procurement budget is particularly damaging as it may disrupt the payment schedule for committed liabilities under multi-year contracts, and the Ministry of Finance has created a special provision called the Demand for Supplementary Grants on a Net Basis to accommodate urgent additional requirements without requiring a surrender of savings from other heads. The Controller General of Defence Accounts introduced an online budget monitoring system in 2021-22 under which the monthly expenditure of each formation is visible in real time to all superior financial authorities, and this transparency has partially addressed the problem of uneven expenditure distribution across the financial year by enabling early identification of under-spending formations and timely remedial action. The Parliamentary Standing Committee on Defence has repeatedly expressed concern about the year-end rush in defence capital expenditure, noting that rushed procurement often results in cost overruns, quality compromises, and the award of contracts to vendors who have not undergone adequate technical evaluation, and has recommended the adoption of multi-year budgeting for major capital acquisition programmes as a structural remedy. The Ministry of Defence, in its response to the Standing Committee's recommendations, has acknowledged the systemic nature of the problem and has proposed the introduction of two-year rolling budget authorisations for capital procurement of items with long production lead times, thereby allowing formations to commit funds in one financial year for delivery and payment in the next, without the risk of lapsing.
