The Army Act 1950 constitutes the foundational legal framework governing discipline, conduct, and administration of justice for personnel subject to military law within the Union of India. The Act extends to officers, junior commissioned officers, and other ranks enrolled in the regular Army, as well as to certain categories of civilians accompanying the armed forces under specified conditions. It classifies offences into civil offences and purely military offences, thereby establishing a dual framework wherein acts punishable under the Indian Penal Code may also be triable under military jurisdiction. The Act ensures that operational exigencies are balanced with legal safeguards, thereby preserving discipline while maintaining procedural fairness. Section 3 of the Act defines key terms, while subsequent sections elaborate upon punishable conduct ranging from insubordination to offences against state security. The relationship between military law and civilian criminal law is carefully structured to prevent jurisdictional conflicts, with provisions allowing for transfer of cases between civil courts and court martial authorities. Amendments to the Act over the years have incorporated modern legal standards, including procedural safeguards and recognition of human rights obligations. The authority to administer the Act is vested in commanding officers, subject to oversight by higher military and governmental authorities. The Act further empowers the Central Government to frame rules for its implementation, ensuring adaptability to evolving defence requirements. In practice, the Army Act 1950 serves as both a disciplinary code and a legal instrument safeguarding the integrity of the armed forces.

Court martial proceedings under the Army Act are categorized into General Court Martial, District Court Martial, and Summary Court Martial, each differing in jurisdiction, composition, and sentencing authority. A General Court Martial is convened by the Central Government or authorized military commanders and is empowered to try any offence under the Act, including those punishable by death. It comprises a minimum of five officers, and its proceedings are conducted under the supervision of a Judge Advocate who ensures adherence to legal standards. A District Court Martial, typically convened by officers of the rank of Brigadier or equivalent, handles offences of a less severe nature and has limited sentencing powers compared to a General Court Martial. The Summary Court Martial is presided over by the commanding officer of the accused and is intended for swift disposal of minor offences, particularly in operational areas. The procedural safeguards in Summary Court Martial are comparatively limited, reflecting the need for expediency in field conditions. Each type of court martial operates within clearly defined legal boundaries, ensuring proportionality between the offence and the forum of trial. The convening authority plays a critical role in determining the appropriate type of court martial based on the gravity of the offence. The composition of the court is structured to maintain impartiality and operational efficiency. The differences in punishment powers among the three types ensure a graded system of military justice.

The procedure for court martial commences with the framing of charges against the accused under the applicable sections of the Army Act. A charge sheet is prepared and served upon the accused, detailing the nature of the offence and the evidence supporting the allegations. The accused is entitled to be informed of the charges in a language understood by him, thereby ensuring transparency and fairness. The role of the Judge Advocate General’s branch becomes prominent at this stage, as it provides legal oversight and ensures compliance with procedural requirements. During the trial, evidence is presented by the prosecution, and the accused is given full opportunity to cross-examine witnesses and present a defence. The rules of evidence, though not identical to those in civilian courts, adhere to principles of natural justice. Upon conclusion of the trial, the court records its findings and pronounces the sentence, subject to confirmation by the competent authority. The confirmation process acts as a safeguard against arbitrary decisions and ensures uniformity in sentencing. The accused has the right to submit a petition against the findings and sentence, which is examined by higher authorities. Appeals may also be filed before the Armed Forces Tribunal under prescribed conditions. The entire procedure reflects a balance between maintaining discipline and upholding legal rights.

The Armed Forces Tribunal was established under the Armed Forces Tribunal Act 2007 to provide an independent judicial forum for adjudication of disputes relating to service matters and appeals arising from court martial proceedings. The Tribunal comprises judicial members, typically retired High Court judges, and administrative members drawn from senior ranks of the armed forces. Its Principal Bench is located in New Delhi, with regional benches established across the country to ensure accessibility. The jurisdiction of the Tribunal extends to all service matters including promotion, seniority, pension, and disciplinary action. Applications before the Tribunal are filed in prescribed formats, such as TA/0234/2021/Delhi, indicating the transfer application number and bench jurisdiction. The Tribunal follows a structured procedure, including admission, notice to respondents, filing of replies, and final hearing. Timelines for disposal are intended to be expeditious, though practical delays may occur due to case load. The Tribunal has the power to grant interim relief, including stay of adverse orders. Decisions of the Tribunal are binding, subject to judicial review by the Supreme Court under Article 136 of the Constitution. The establishment of the Tribunal has significantly reduced the burden on civil courts in military matters.

Disability pension disputes arise when there is disagreement between defence personnel and authorities regarding the classification of disability as attributable to or aggravated by military service. Medical boards constituted under service regulations assess the nature and extent of disability, categorizing it into attributable, aggravated, or neither. This classification directly impacts eligibility for disability pension under relevant pension regulations. The Ministry of Defence often contests claims where the disability is deemed not connected to service conditions. The Armed Forces Tribunal has, in numerous cases, adopted a liberal interpretation in favour of personnel, emphasizing the benefit of doubt principle. Case precedents have established that if a disease manifests during service without prior indication, it may be presumed attributable unless proven otherwise. The Tribunal has also scrutinized procedural lapses in medical board assessments. Legal representation in such cases often involves detailed examination of service records and medical evidence. The Ministry is required to justify its position with documented evidence, failing which decisions are overturned. These disputes highlight the evolving jurisprudence in military pension law. The outcomes significantly influence policy revisions within the Ministry.

Service matter appeals before the Armed Forces Tribunal commonly involve issues such as wrongful termination, denial of promotion, or reversion in rank. Applicants challenge administrative decisions on grounds of violation of natural justice, procedural irregularity, or mala fide intent. The Tribunal examines service records, confidential reports, and relevant policy documents to determine the validity of the claim. The Ministry of Defence is represented by legal officers who defend the administrative action taken. Timelines for such cases vary, though efforts are made to ensure timely adjudication. In many instances, the Tribunal has directed reinstatement or reconsideration of cases where procedural lapses were identified. The balance between maintaining discipline and ensuring fairness is a recurring theme in such decisions. Outcomes often depend on the strength of documentary evidence and adherence to prescribed procedures. The Tribunal’s decisions serve as precedents influencing future administrative practices. These appeals form a significant portion of the Tribunal’s caseload.

Pension commutation allows retired defence personnel to receive a portion of their pension as a lump sum, subject to prescribed limits and conditions. The commuted portion is calculated based on actuarial tables and is deducted from monthly pension payments. Restoration of the commuted portion occurs after a period of fifteen years, as per prevailing rules. Disputes often arise regarding incorrect calculation or delayed restoration. Legal remedies are available through the Armed Forces Tribunal, which examines compliance with pension regulations. The Ministry of Defence is required to maintain accurate records to avoid discrepancies. The Tribunal has, in several cases, directed recalculation and payment of arrears. Pension commutation rules are periodically revised to reflect economic conditions and policy changes. Awareness among personnel regarding their entitlements remains a critical factor. These disputes underscore the importance of transparency in pension administration.

Absence Without Leave and desertion are distinct offences under the Army Act, each carrying different legal consequences. AWOL refers to unauthorized absence for a limited period, whereas desertion implies intention to permanently abandon service. The period of absence and circumstances determine the classification of the offence. Desertion is considered a grave offence and may attract severe punishment including imprisonment. Trial procedures for such offences follow established court martial processes. Amnesty provisions may be invoked in certain cases to encourage return to service. Reinstatement depends on the nature of the offence and the individual’s service record. The distinction between AWOL and desertion is critical in determining legal outcomes. Commanding officers play a key role in initiating proceedings. These provisions ensure discipline while allowing scope for rehabilitation in appropriate cases.

Internal Complaints Committees are constituted at unit and formation levels to receive and investigate complaints. The procedure involves confidential inquiry, examination of evidence, and submission of findings within prescribed timelines. Disciplinary action may include administrative measures or trial under the Army Act. The interface with civilian law becomes relevant in cases involving criminal offences. The Ministry has issued guidelines to ensure sensitivity and fairness in handling such cases. Awareness programs are conducted to prevent incidents and encourage reporting. The legal framework aims to balance discipline with protection of individual rights. Decisions are subject to review by higher authorities. The evolving nature of such cases reflects broader societal changes.

The Supreme Court has emphasized the need for fairness while respecting the unique requirements of military discipline. Writ petitions often challenge findings or sentences on legal grounds.
