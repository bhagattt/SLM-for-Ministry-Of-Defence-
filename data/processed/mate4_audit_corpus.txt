The Comptroller and Auditor General of India conducts audit of the Ministry of Defence under the authority conferred by the CAG’s Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service Act 1971, which mandates examination of all expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India. The audit covers both compliance and performance aspects, ensuring that funds are utilized with due regard to economy, efficiency, and effectiveness. Audit teams are deployed within defence establishments with prior intimation, and they examine records including procurement files, sanction orders, and contract agreements. The audit process includes entry conferences, field audit, draft paras, and exit conferences with departmental representatives. Observations are compiled into audit reports which are submitted to the President of India and subsequently laid before Parliament. The Ministry of Defence is required to furnish replies to audit observations within stipulated timelines, generally within six to eight weeks. Persistent irregularities may be escalated in subsequent audit reports until compliance is achieved. The audit also includes test checks of high-value transactions and thematic reviews of procurement systems. Coordination between defence accounts departments and audit teams is essential for smooth conduct of audit. The audit findings significantly influence policy corrections and administrative reforms within the Ministry.

The Defence Procurement Procedure 2020 provides the regulatory framework governing all capital acquisitions undertaken by the Ministry of Defence and its services. Compliance with DPP provisions is mandatory at every stage of procurement, from acceptance of necessity to contract conclusion. Each stage involves defined approval authorities, documentation requirements, and financial scrutiny. The Defence Acquisition Council, chaired by the Raksha Mantri, exercises oversight over major procurements and ensures adherence to policy guidelines. Deviations from prescribed procedures require explicit approval with recorded justification. Audit scrutiny often focuses on whether procurement categories have been correctly applied and whether evaluation processes were conducted transparently. Non-compliance with DPP provisions may result in financial irregularities and accountability of responsible officers. Documentation such as technical evaluation reports, commercial negotiation committee minutes, and contract agreements must be maintained meticulously. The procedure also mandates timelines to prevent undue delays in acquisition. The compliance framework ensures transparency, accountability, and competitiveness in defence procurement.

Split purchasing refers to the practice of dividing a single procurement requirement into multiple smaller orders to circumvent financial thresholds and approval requirements. This practice is considered a violation of General Financial Rules and procurement guidelines as it undermines transparency and competitive bidding. The CAG identifies such instances by analyzing procurement patterns, vendor repetition, and timing of orders. Audit reports have highlighted cases where identical items were procured through multiple orders within a short period to avoid open tender requirements. Such actions are treated as deliberate attempts to bypass established procedures and attract disciplinary action. Financial implications include inflated costs and reduced accountability. Officers responsible for such decisions may face administrative and financial penalties. The detection of split purchasing relies on data analytics and comparative analysis of procurement records. Preventive measures include strict adherence to procurement planning and centralized approval systems. The issue remains a recurring concern in defence audits.

Single vendor procurement is permissible under specific conditions where only one vendor is capable of supplying the required equipment or where operational urgency justifies limited sourcing. However, such cases require detailed justification, including certification of single vendor status and approval from competent authority. Documentation must include market survey reports and reasons for absence of competition. The CAG scrutinizes such cases to ensure that the justification is genuine and not fabricated to favour a particular vendor. Instances have been reported where single vendor situations were created artificially by restrictive specifications. Audit observations often recommend re-tendering or cancellation of such procurements. Proper documentation is essential to withstand audit scrutiny. Transparency in specification formulation and vendor selection is critical. The Ministry has issued guidelines to minimize single vendor situations. Compliance ensures fair competition and optimal utilization of resources.

Emergency procurement provisions allow bypassing of standard tendering procedures under exceptional circumstances such as war or urgent operational requirements. Such procurements must be justified with clear evidence of urgency and approved by competent authority. Financial limits are prescribed beyond which higher-level approvals are mandatory. Post-procurement documentation must include detailed justification, comparative analysis, and certification of reasonableness of price. The CAG examines whether emergency provisions were invoked appropriately or misused to avoid competitive bidding. Audit findings have indicated instances where emergency procurement was used for routine purchases. Such misuse attracts serious observations and accountability. The Ministry has reinforced guidelines to prevent abuse of emergency powers. Proper documentation and approval hierarchy are critical for compliance. These provisions ensure flexibility while maintaining accountability.

Advance payments to vendors are governed by provisions of the General Financial Rules which restrict such payments to exceptional cases with adequate safeguards. Typically, advance payment is limited to a specified percentage of contract value and must be backed by bank guarantees of equivalent amount. The purpose is to protect government interest in case of non-performance by the vendor. Audit observations have identified cases where advances were released without obtaining valid bank guarantees. Such practices expose the government to financial risk. Recovery of advance payments becomes difficult in case of vendor default. Compliance requires strict adherence to contractual terms and financial safeguards. The Ministry has issued instructions to ensure verification of guarantees before release of funds. Monitoring mechanisms are essential to track utilization of advances. Proper compliance minimizes financial exposure.

Make in India policy in defence procurement mandates minimum local content requirements to promote indigenous manufacturing. Vendors are required to declare and certify the percentage of local content in their products. Audit verification involves examination of supporting documents such as bills of materials and manufacturing processes. Instances of false declaration of local content have been detected, leading to penalties and blacklisting of vendors. Offset obligations for foreign vendors are also monitored to ensure compliance with policy objectives. Audit mechanisms track fulfillment of offset commitments over the contract period. Non-compliance results in financial penalties and contractual action. The policy aims to reduce dependence on imports and strengthen domestic industry. Proper implementation requires coordination between procurement agencies and industry. Compliance ensures alignment with national objectives.

Asset write-off procedures involve condemnation of obsolete or unserviceable equipment through a Board of Officers constituted as per prescribed norms. The board assesses the condition of assets and recommends disposal or write-off based on technical evaluation. Approval authority depends on the value of the asset being written off. Proper documentation including survey reports and condemnation certificates is mandatory. Audit findings have highlighted cases of irregular write-offs without proper assessment. Disposal of scrap must be conducted through authorized channels to ensure transparency. Financial accounting of write-offs must be reflected accurately in records. Non-compliance may result in loss to the exchequer. The Ministry has issued guidelines to streamline condemnation procedures. Proper compliance ensures accountability in asset management.

Maintenance of procurement files is critical for audit compliance as it provides evidence of decision-making process. Each file must contain noting sheets, approvals, comparative statements, and contract documents. Incomplete documentation may lead to adverse audit observations. Electronic file management systems have been introduced to improve record keeping. Audit teams examine whether proper procedures were followed at each stage. Missing documents raise questions about transparency and accountability. The Ministry has emphasized the importance of proper file maintenance. Training programs are conducted to improve documentation practices. Compliance ensures smooth audit and reduces risk of objections. Proper records also support legal defence in case of disputes.

Conflict of interest in procurement is governed by guidelines issued by the Central Vigilance Commission which require disclosure of any personal or financial interest by officials involved in procurement decisions. Members of procurement committees must certify absence of conflict before participating in evaluation. Cases have been reported where officials had undisclosed links with vendors, leading to biased decisions. Audit scrutiny includes examination of declarations and background checks. Violations result in disciplinary action and possible cancellation of contracts. Transparency in committee composition is essential for fairness. The Ministry has strengthened vigilance mechanisms to detect such conflicts. Regular audits help identify potential risks. Compliance promotes integrity in procurement processes.

Post-retirement employment of defence officials with private vendors is regulated by cooling-off period rules to prevent conflict of interest. Retired officials are required to obtain prior approval before accepting employment with companies dealing with defence procurement. The Central Vigilance Commission monitors compliance with these rules. Cases of violation have been reported where officials joined vendors immediately after retirement. Such instances raise concerns about undue influence and insider information. Audit observations emphasize strict enforcement of cooling-off periods. The Ministry has issued instructions to track post-retirement engagements. Non-compliance may lead to penalties and reputational damage. Transparency in such matters is essential. Compliance ensures ethical standards.

Maintenance contracts for defence equipment are subject to rules requiring periodic re-tendering to ensure competitiveness. Renewal of contracts without competition is permitted only under specific conditions with proper justification. Audit findings have highlighted cases where contracts were extended repeatedly without fresh tendering. Such practices result in higher costs and reduced efficiency. Proper documentation of justification is required for audit compliance. The Ministry has issued guidelines to ensure timely re-tendering. Monitoring mechanisms track contract duration and renewal timelines. Compliance ensures value for money. Audit scrutiny helps identify inefficiencies. Proper practices improve operational readiness.

Acceptance of gifts and hospitality by defence officials is regulated under Central Vigilance Commission guidelines which prescribe limits and disclosure requirements. Officials are prohibited from accepting gifts from vendors involved in procurement processes. Any gift above prescribed value must be reported and deposited as per rules. Audit observations have identified instances of undisclosed gifts influencing decisions. Such actions attract disciplinary proceedings. Transparency in interactions with vendors is essential. The Ministry conducts awareness programs on ethical conduct. Compliance ensures integrity in official dealings. Monitoring mechanisms detect violations. Strict enforcement deters misconduct.

Infrastructure projects within the Ministry of Defence require detailed cost estimation and approval before execution. Projects are executed through agencies such as Military Engineering Services under prescribed procedures. Cost overruns require approval from competent authority with justification. Audit findings have highlighted cases of inflated estimates and delays leading to increased costs. Proper tendering procedures must be followed for civil works. Documentation of approvals and expenditure is essential for audit. Monitoring of project progress ensures timely completion. The Ministry has introduced measures to control cost escalation. Compliance ensures efficient utilization of resources. Audit scrutiny improves project management.

Compliance audit trail requires documentation of all decisions and checks performed before procurement or financial transactions. Officers are required to record compliance with relevant rules and guidelines in file noting. Such documentation protects officers during future audit scrutiny. Pre-decision checks include verification of budget availability, approval authority, and procedural compliance. Audit teams rely on these records to assess adherence to rules. Absence of proper audit trail leads to adverse observations. The concept of audit trail has gained importance with digitization of records. The Ministry encourages use of standardized formats for compliance documentation. Proper audit trail enhances transparency and accountability. It also supports decision-making processes within the organization.
