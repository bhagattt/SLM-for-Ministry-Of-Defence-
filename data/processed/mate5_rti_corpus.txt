The Right to Information Act, 2005, hereinafter referred to as the Act, came into force on the twelfth day of October 2005 and casts a statutory obligation on all public authorities, including the Ministry of Defence and its attached and subordinate offices, to furnish information to citizens of India upon receipt of a valid application accompanied by the prescribed application fee. The Ministry of Defence, being one of the largest public authorities under the Government of India, receives applications from a wide cross-section of citizens including serving and retired defence personnel, their family members, contractors, journalists, researchers, and members of the general public. Section 2(h) of the Act defines "public authority" to include any authority or body established or constituted by or under the Constitution, or by any other law made by Parliament, and the Ministry of Defence and all formations under it, including the three Services Headquarters, the Department of Defence Production, the Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare, and the Department of Defence Research and Development, fall squarely within this definition. The prescribed application fee under the Right to Information (Regulation of Fee and Cost) Rules, 2005 is rupees ten, payable by way of cash against proper receipt, demand draft, banker's cheque, or Indian Postal Order drawn in favour of the Accounts Officer of the public authority concerned. Persons below the poverty line, as certified by the competent authority of the respective State Government or Union Territory, are exempt from payment of the application fee under Rule 6 of the said Rules. The Ministry of Defence has organized its RTI machinery through the designation of Central Public Information Officers at the level of Under Secretary or above in each Division and Wing of the Ministry's Secretariat, and separate CPIOs have been designated for the Department of Defence, Department of Defence Production, Department of Defence Research and Development, and Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare. In addition to the CPIOs at the Ministry headquarters, each of the Service Headquarters — Army Headquarters, Naval Headquarters, and Air Headquarters — has its own RTI cell with designated CPIOs and Appellate Authorities functioning under the broad administrative supervision of the Ministry. The Directorate General of Resettlement, the Kendriya Sainik Board, the Armed Forces Tribunal Registry, and other bodies under the Ministry have independently designated their own CPIOs in accordance with Section 5(1) of the Act. As per the Annual Report data compiled by the Central Information Commission, the Ministry of Defence collectively receives in excess of twenty-five thousand RTI applications annually across all its constituent departments and formations, making it one of the top five public authorities in terms of RTI volume at the Central Government level. The Ministry of Defence RTI Portal, accessible at mod.gov.in, provides applicants with the facility of online submission of RTI applications through the RTI Online Portal maintained by the Department of Personnel and Training, Government of India, which has substantially improved the accessibility and tracking of applications.

The thirty-day timeline prescribed under Section 7(1) of the Act commences from the date on which the application is received by the concerned Central Public Information Officer of the Ministry of Defence, and not from the date on which it was posted or submitted through any intermediary channel. Where an application has been submitted to a public authority other than the one which holds the information, the CPIO of the receiving authority is obligated under Section 6(3) to transfer the application to the concerned public authority within five days of receipt, and the thirty-day period for response shall be computed from the date of receipt by the public authority to which the application is transferred. A valid application under the Act must be in writing or through electronic means in English, Hindi, or the official language of the area in which the application is being filed, and must contain sufficient particulars to enable the CPIO to identify and locate the information being sought, along with proof of payment of the prescribed fee or a valid below poverty line certificate. Where the application does not specify the category of information sought with adequate clarity, the CPIO may seek clarification from the applicant, though such seeking of clarification does not automatically extend the thirty-day period unless the applicant's response to the clarification is awaited. The Central Information Commission has consistently held, in a series of orders including CIC/SA/A/2017/000234 and CIC/MoD/A/2018/151243, that the CPIO cannot reject an application merely on the ground of its being vague and must make reasonable efforts to identify the information sought from the context of the application. The consequence of failing to respond within the thirty-day period is that the CPIO is deemed under Section 7(2) to have refused the application, and the applicant acquires the immediate right to prefer a first appeal under Section 19(1) of the Act. Section 20(1) of the Act empowers the Central Information Commission to impose a penalty on the CPIO at the rate of rupees two hundred and fifty per day of delay, subject to a maximum of rupees twenty-five thousand, where the Commission finds that the CPIO has without reasonable cause failed to comply with the provisions of the Act. The penalty under Section 20(1) is in addition to any disciplinary action that may be recommended by the Commission against the erring CPIO under Section 20(2), and in cases involving the Ministry of Defence, such disciplinary recommendations are forwarded to the Secretary, Department of Defence or the relevant Service Headquarters for action. The deemed refusal provision has been invoked in numerous cases against MoD CPIOs, and the Commission has noted in CIC/MoD/A/2019/162741 that systemic delays in the Ministry of Defence in RTI responses, particularly in matters relating to service record corrections and pension arrears, warrant institutional rather than merely individual corrective measures. The Ministry of Defence has issued standing instructions to all CPIOs through O.M. No. RTI-1/2020/D(RTI) dated 15th March 2020 directing them to maintain a register of RTI applications received and to ensure that responses are dispatched within twenty-five days so as to allow sufficient time for delivery before expiry of the statutory period.

Section 8(1) of the Right to Information Act, 2005 enumerates ten categories of information which are exempt from disclosure, and the Ministry of Defence routinely invokes several of these exemptions given the sensitive nature of its functions. Section 8(1)(a) exempts information the disclosure of which would prejudicially affect the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security, strategic, scientific or economic interests of the State, relations with foreign States, or lead to incitement of an offence. In the context of the Ministry of Defence, this exemption is most frequently applied to information concerning operational deployments, force levels, weapons specifications, communications infrastructure, and strategic plans, and the CPIO must record specific reasons in the file as to why each element of the information sought falls within this exemption. Section 8(1)(b) exempts information expressly forbidden by any court of law or tribunal or the disclosure of which may constitute contempt of court, and MoD CPIOs have invoked this in matters where information relates to pending litigation or to material covered by judicial orders. Section 8(1)(c) exempts information the disclosure of which would cause a breach of privilege of Parliament or State Legislature, and while this exemption has limited application in MoD's day-to-day RTI work, it may arise in contexts involving correspondence with Parliamentary Committees. Section 8(1)(d) provides protection to information including commercial confidence, trade secrets, or intellectual property the disclosure of which would harm the competitive position of a third party, unless the competent authority is satisfied that larger public interest warrants disclosure, and this exemption is centrally relevant to defence procurement and DRDO matters as elaborated in subsequent paragraphs. Section 8(1)(e) exempts information available to a person in their fiduciary relationship unless the competent authority is satisfied that larger public interest warrants disclosure, and the Ministry has invoked this in limited contexts involving communications between MoD and its bankers or financial advisors. Section 8(1)(f) exempts information received in confidence from foreign governments, and MoD applies this to intelligence-sharing arrangements, joint exercises, and diplomatic communications under bilateral defence agreements. Section 8(1)(g) exempts information the disclosure of which would endanger the life or physical safety of any person or identify the source of information or assistance given in confidence for law enforcement or security purposes, and in MoD's context this extends to protection of the identities of informers, undercover personnel, and individuals who have provided intelligence inputs. Section 8(1)(h) exempts information that would impede the process of investigation or apprehension or prosecution of offenders, and MoD has invoked this in RTIs relating to ongoing court martial proceedings and vigilance investigations. Section 8(1)(i) exempts Cabinet papers including records of deliberations of the Council of Ministers, Secretaries, and other officers, provided that decisions and material related to implementing decisions are disclosed once they have been taken, subject to the protections of other exemptions. Section 8(1)(j) exempts information which relates to personal information the disclosure of which has no relationship to any public activity or interest, or which would cause unwarranted invasion of the privacy of an individual unless the Central Public Information Officer or the appellate authority is satisfied that the larger public interest justifies the disclosure, and this exemption has extensive application in MoD matters involving service records, medical histories, and personnel files of defence personnel.

The exemption under Section 8(1)(a) is the most frequently invoked by the Ministry of Defence and its associated bodies, and its application requires the CPIO to exercise considered judgment rather than reflexive invocation of the national security shield. The Central Information Commission has, in a series of landmark orders, drawn a clear distinction between information that genuinely threatens the security or strategic interests of the State and information which merely relates to the armed forces or defence establishment but does not carry any operational sensitivity. In CIC/MoD/A/2016/004521, the Commission held that information regarding the number of personnel posted in a particular cantonment area does not per se threaten national security and directed the Ministry to disclose aggregate strength figures while redacting unit-level deployments. In CIC/MoD/A/2018/152300, the Commission held that the composition of committees convened to examine service matters, being an administrative rather than operational matter, could not be protected under Section 8(1)(a) and directed disclosure. The Ministry of Defence has in several instances applied Section 8(1)(a) to withhold information about accidents involving defence equipment, delays in procurement projects, and financial irregularities, and the Commission has consistently held that embarrassment to the institution does not constitute a national security risk warranting the exemption. The CIC has further held in CIC/SA/A/2017/000891 that the public interest override contained in the proviso to Section 8(1) permits disclosure even of otherwise exempt information where the benefit to the public of knowing the information outweighs the harm from disclosure, and that in cases involving loss of life or allegations of negligence, this public interest balance tilts strongly in favour of disclosure. The Ministry's standing instructions on RTI responses, issued through RTI Cell, Department of Defence, specifically caution CPIOs that Section 8(1)(a) must not be used as a blanket refusal and that the specific element of information which triggers the exemption must be identified and articulated. Where information pertaining to strategic plans, weapons testing, or intelligence assessments is genuinely sought, the Ministry follows an internal clearance procedure whereby the CPIO consults the Security Division of the Ministry and obtains a written assessment of the security sensitivity of the information before invoking the exemption. The Commission in CIC/MoD/A/2020/103456 directed the Ministry to disclose information about the broad financial parameters of a completed and publicly announced procurement, holding that once a contract has been executed and announced, the commercial and strategic sensitivity of price information diminishes substantially. The consistent thread in CIC jurisprudence on MoD Section 8(1)(a) cases is that the exemption is to be construed narrowly and applied to specific information rather than whole categories of records, and that documents which mix sensitive and non-sensitive information must be subjected to the severability analysis under Section 10 of the Act.

The protection of personal information of serving and retired defence personnel under Section 8(1)(j) of the Act presents one of the most contested areas in MoD RTI jurisprudence. Section 8(1)(j) is structured as a two-pronged test — first, whether the information relates to personal information; and second, whether its disclosure has any relationship to a public activity or interest, or whether disclosure would cause unwarranted invasion of privacy. The Central Information Commission has, in CIC/MoD/A/2017/135672, held that the salary of a public servant, including a defence officer, is information relating to a public function and does not attract the privacy exemption, and accordingly directed the Ministry to disclose the pay scale and allowances drawn by the officer in question. In contrast, the Commission has held that the personal medical records, psychiatric evaluation reports, and domestic life particulars of defence personnel are squarely protected under Section 8(1)(j) and that disclosure of such information would constitute an unwarranted invasion of privacy without serving any legitimate public interest. The Annual Confidential Reports of officers and Jawans, being evaluative documents prepared in a fiduciary administrative context, are generally held to be exempt from disclosure, though the CIC has drawn a distinction between the substantive evaluation which may be protected and procedural information about the ACR process which may be disclosed. Information about the posting and transfer of officers, being an administrative matter with implications for institutional accountability, occupies an intermediate position — the fact of a posting may be disclosable while the operational reasons for the posting may be protected. The Ministry of Defence through its O.M. No. RTI-4/2019/D(RTI) dated 22nd July 2019 has issued instructions to CPIOs to carefully examine the specific personal information sought before invoking Section 8(1)(j), and to note that third-party information procedures under Section 11 must be followed where the personal information relates to a living individual and the CPIO proposes to disclose it. The CIC has in CIC/MoD/A/2019/167823 held that where an RTI application is filed by a defence personnel themselves seeking their own service record, the privacy exemption is not available to the CPIO as the exemption exists for the benefit of the person whose information it is, and that person has chosen to seek the information. In cases involving war widows and next of kin of deceased personnel seeking information about the circumstances of death, injuries, or service records, the Commission has consistently held that legitimate family interest overrides the institutional tendency to withhold and that a broad and liberal interpretation of disclosure is warranted in such cases.

Section 10 of the Right to Information Act, 2005 enshrines the principle of severability, which mandates that where a request for information is refused on the ground that it relates to information which is exempt from disclosure, the CPIO shall provide access to so much of the information as can be severed from the exempt portion and made available without thereby revealing the exempt information. The severability principle imposes an affirmative duty on the CPIO to analyse each document sought and to identify the portions that can be disclosed without compromising the exempt information, rather than withholding entire documents on the basis that a portion of the document attracts an exemption. In MoD's operational context, this means that a procurement file containing both commercially sensitive cost negotiation data protected under Section 8(1)(d) and the names of approved vendors, the broad scope of work, and the administrative correspondence, must be severed to provide access to the non-sensitive portions. The CPIO in preparing a partial disclosure response must ensure that the redaction of sensitive portions is done in a manner that does not permit the applicant to reconstruct the exempt information through inference or deduction, and where such reconstruction is possible, the entire document or page may be withheld with clear reasons recorded. The proper format for a partial disclosure response requires the CPIO to first acknowledge receipt of the application and identify each category of information sought, then to specify for each category whether information is being provided in full, provided in part with specified redactions citing the applicable Section 8(1) subsection, or withheld in full with specific reasons. The CIC has in CIC/MoD/A/2018/159034 emphasized that a response which simply states that the information is exempt and provides no document whatsoever, when a partial disclosure was possible, is a defective response that does not comply with the Act. The response letter must contain the specific words and citations required under Section 7(8) of the Act, including the period within which the applicant may appeal, the particulars of the First Appellate Authority within MoD, and the grounds for refusal of each portion that is withheld. The Ministry of Defence through its RTI Training Manual, last revised in 2021 and circulated to all CPIOs through the Joint Secretary (RTI), prescribes a standard template for partial disclosure responses which requires the CPIO to list the documents examined, the portions disclosed, the portions redacted, and the legal basis for each redaction. Where physical documents are provided in response, portions containing exempt information must be physically blacked out before supply, and where electronic documents are provided, metadata that may reveal sensitive information must also be reviewed and appropriately redacted. The Commission has directed in CIC/MoD/A/2020/102345 that the Ministry develop and implement a standard redaction protocol applicable across all CPIOs to ensure uniformity and to prevent inadvertent disclosure of exempt information or excessive redaction of non-exempt information.

Pension and service record matters constitute a significant proportion of RTI applications received by the Ministry of Defence, with the Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare, the Office of the Principal Controller of Defence Accounts (Pension), and the Kendriya Sainik Board being among the busiest RTI responding entities under the MoD umbrella. The fundamental principle governing pension-related RTI disclosures is that the pension of a public servant is a public document — it is calculated on the basis of publicly known rules, approved pay commissions, and statutory entitlements — and therefore the fact of a pension entitlement and the basis for its calculation must be disclosed upon request. The CIC in CIC/DESW/A/2017/134562 held that the Pension Payment Order issued to a retired soldier is a document that the pensioner is himself entitled to and that the Department may not refuse to provide a copy thereof to the pensioner or his legal heir under RTI, particularly where administrative channels have failed to provide the document. In cases where the quantum of pension is disputed and the pensioner or war widow seeks the detailed computation sheet including the qualifying service calculation, the basic pay reckoned, the average emoluments, and the weightage applied, the Commission has held that all such computational details are required to be disclosed as they pertain to the public function of pension sanction and not to any private information of the individual. The Ministry of Defence and the PCDA (Pensions) at Allahabad have been directed in numerous CIC orders, including CIC/DESW/A/2019/145234 and CIC/DESW/A/2020/113467, to disclose the specific government orders, circulars, and policy decisions on the basis of which pension calculations were made or revised, as such orders are themselves in the public domain or ought to be suo motu disclosed under Section 4 of the Act. The matter of disability pension and its determination involves medical boards whose proceedings have a dual character — the medical assessment portions may attract Section 8(1)(j) as personal medical information, while the category assigned for pension purposes and the entitlement decision are administrative determinations that must be disclosed. War widows filing RTI applications to ascertain whether their deceased husbands' service records correctly reflect battle casualties or attributable causes of death have been consistently supported by CIC orders directing disclosure of the relevant administrative correspondence, and the Commission has taken note in several cases of MoD's tendency to cite security exemptions in matters having no bearing on current operations. The Entitlement Rules for Casualties — Pensionary Awards, 2008 and the Regulations for the Army, Navy, and Air Force relating to pension entitlement are statutory instruments whose provisions must be disclosed upon request as they are legislative in character. The Ministry has been directed through CIC/DESW/A/2021/156789 to post on its website all policy circulars and administrative instructions relating to pension calculation, revision, and enhancement that are not per se sensitive, in fulfilment of its proactive disclosure obligations.

The Ministry of Defence is one of the largest procuring entities of the Government of India, and RTI applications concerning defence procurement — covering everything from large platforms like ships, aircraft, and armoured vehicles to small stores, ammunition, and rations — raise some of the most complex disclosure questions under the Act. The Defence Acquisition Procedure, currently in its 2020 iteration, governs the procurement cycle from categorisation through final delivery, and the RTI obligations of MoD vary at different stages of this cycle. In the pre-tendering stage, information about the broad capability requirements that have been approved for procurement may in certain cases be disclosable as they may be publicly known through official announcements, Request for Information notices, or Parliamentary statements, while the detailed qualitative requirements that form the basis of tendering may attract protection under Section 8(1)(a) or Section 8(1)(d). Once a tender has been issued and is in the public domain, basic information about its existence, its broad scope, and the categories of eligible vendors becomes freely disclosable and the Ministry cannot plead any exemption in respect of information that has been itself publicly released. Section 8(1)(d), which protects commercial confidence, trade secrets, and intellectual property the disclosure of which would harm the competitive position of a third party, is the primary basis on which MoD withholds the detailed technical and financial bids submitted by competing vendors, and the CIC has consistently upheld this exemption in respect of third-party bid documents while directing the Ministry to disclose its own evaluation methodology and the broad basis of its final selection. The Commission in CIC/MoD/A/2018/150123 held that the comparative statement of tenders and the final L-1 determination, with commercially sensitive per-unit prices of unsuccessful bidders redacted, should be disclosed to enable public scrutiny of the procurement process, and that the total contract value awarded to the successful bidder is not exempt once the contract has been executed. The third-party notice procedure under Section 11 of the Act must be followed by the CPIO where an applicant seeks information that relates to or has been supplied by a third party in confidence, including information about the technical specifications or proprietary processes of a defence equipment manufacturer, and the CPIO must give the third party twenty-one days to make a representation before the disclosure decision is taken. The CIC in CIC/MoD/A/2019/163412 noted with concern that MoD CPIOs had been routinely rejecting procurement-related RTIs without following the Section 11 procedure and directed that all cases involving third-party commercial information must mandatorily comply with the notification procedure. Post-contract award, information about the execution status, delivery schedules, and penalty clauses in contracts for essential defence stores occupies a public interest dimension and the Commission has in appropriate cases directed disclosure of contract performance information in the public interest. Offset obligations under the Defence Acquisition Procedure, being a matter of national industrial policy, are subject to a higher degree of transparency and the Commission has directed in CIC/MoD/A/2020/107823 that information about offset discharge certificates and compliance status be disclosed, subject to redaction of commercially sensitive technical details.

Court martial proceedings, being the primary instrument of military discipline and justice under the Army Act, 1950, the Navy Act, 1957, and the Air Force Act, 1950, have been a recurring subject of RTI applications seeking information about the charges framed, the evidence adduced, the findings recorded, and the punishments awarded. The Ministry of Defence has consistently taken the position that court martial records are exempt from disclosure under multiple provisions of the Act, including Section 8(1)(g) on the ground of endangering the safety of witnesses and informants, Section 8(1)(h) on the ground of impeding ongoing proceedings, and Section 8(1)(j) on the ground of personal privacy of the accused and witnesses. The Central Information Commission has, however, drawn important distinctions in CIC/MoD/A/2016/002341 between records of completed court martial proceedings and those of ongoing proceedings — while an ongoing general court martial may attract the Section 8(1)(h) exemption, a completed and decided court martial, particularly one involving an officer above the rank of Colonel or equivalent, has a public dimension that warrants transparency. The Commission has held in CIC/MoD/A/2017/127634 that the summary of evidence and the charge sheet in a court martial involving alleged financial irregularities or misuse of public resources carry a public interest character, and that the Ministry's blanket refusal on privacy grounds was not warranted where the accused was a senior officer acting in a public capacity. The Armed Forces Tribunal, having been constituted under the Armed Forces Tribunal Act, 2007, maintains records of appeals against court martial findings, and these Tribunal records are in a distinct legal position from the underlying court martial records — being judicial records of a statutory tribunal, they are subject to a presumption of public access subject to applicable law. The High Courts have also addressed the interplay between RTI and military justice, and in WP (C) 14523/2018 before the Delhi High Court, the Court held that court martial proceedings are not automatically exempt and that the Ministry must demonstrate specific harm before withholding records. The Ministry of Defence RTI Manual for Service Headquarters instructs CPIOs receiving RTI applications about court martial proceedings to consult the Judge Advocate General's Branch before issuing a response and to assess whether the proceedings have been concluded, whether the information sought would identify witnesses or intelligence sources, and whether the accused has been formally acquitted or convicted, as these factors bear on the applicability of the exemptions. Information about the overall statistics of courts martial — number convened per year, categories of offences, conviction rates — has been directed to be disclosed by the Commission in CIC/MoD/A/2018/151098 as such aggregate information does not reveal any specific individual's private information and serves the legitimate public interest in understanding the functioning of military justice.

The Defence Research and Development Organisation, functioning under the Department of Defence Research and Development which is a constituent department of the Ministry of Defence, presents a uniquely complex RTI compliance landscape owing to the dual character of its work — which combines classified strategic research and development, sensitive technology acquisition, and commercially sensitive intellectual property development with published academic and scientific output and publicly announced achievements. DRDO has designated CPIOs at its headquarters in New Delhi as well as at each of its fifty-odd laboratories and establishments across India, and the RTI applications received by DRDO collectively number in the thousands annually, covering a wide range from queries about employment and recruitment to requests for information about specific weapon systems under development. The primary exemptions invoked by DRDO in respect of project-related information are Section 8(1)(a) on national security grounds, Section 8(1)(d) on commercial confidence and intellectual property grounds, and Section 8(1)(e) on fiduciary relationship grounds where the research involves collaborations with foreign entities under classified agreements. The Central Information Commission has in CIC/DRDO/A/2017/140236 held that DRDO cannot invoke Section 8(1)(a) as a matter of course for all project information and must demonstrate with specificity how the disclosure of the particular information sought would prejudicially affect the security or strategic interests of the State. In matters involving DRDO's recruitment processes, training programmes, organizational structure, administrative expenditure, and published research findings, the Commission has consistently directed full disclosure and has noted in several orders that DRDO's tendency to classify all information by association with defence research does not satisfy the specific harm test required by Section 8(1)(a). Information about patents registered by DRDO in its own name, being published patents whose contents are in the public domain through the patent database of the Controller General of Patents, cannot be withheld under any exemption, and the Commission in CIC/DRDO/A/2019/161234 directed DRDO to provide patent publication numbers and application dates to the RTI applicant. Where DRDO has entered into technology transfer agreements with private sector entities for manufacturing defence equipment, the terms of such agreements occupy an intermediate position — the broad scope and the name of the technology licensee may be disclosable while the commercially sensitive technical parameters and financial consideration may be protected under Section 8(1)(d) with prior notice to the third party under Section 11. DRDO's annual technology development fund expenditure, being an appropriation of public money approved by Parliament through the Defence Budget, must be disclosed at the level of broad programme categories, and the Commission has directed such disclosure in CIC/DRDO/A/2020/109876 while upholding DRDO's right to protect the specific project-level breakdown on security grounds where adequately justified. The intellectual property generated by DRDO scientists, including copyrights, design registrations, and know-how transferred to industry, occupies the intersection of Section 8(1)(d) and Section 8(2), which clarifies that the exemption from disclosure does not apply where the information relates to matters in which the competent authority is satisfied that the larger public interest warrants disclosure, and the Commission has directed disclosure of aggregated IP statistics while upholding project-level confidentiality.

The first appeal mechanism under Section 19(1) of the Right to Information Act, 2005 provides an essential intra-department remedy for applicants who are dissatisfied with the response of the CPIO, whether because the information has been wholly or partially denied, because the fee levied for providing information is considered excessive, or because no response has been received within the statutory period. In the Ministry of Defence, the First Appellate Authority for each Department is an officer senior in rank to the CPIO, typically at the level of Joint Secretary or Additional Secretary in the Ministry Secretariat and at equivalent levels in the Service Headquarters, and their names and contact details are required to be disclosed on the Ministry's website under the Section 4 proactive disclosure obligation. The first appeal must be filed within thirty days of the date of receipt of the CPIO's response, or within thirty days of the expiry of the thirty-day period for response where no response has been received, though the First Appellate Authority has the power under Section 19(1) proviso to admit an appeal filed after the thirty-day period if sufficient cause for the delay is shown. The First Appellate Authority is required under Section 19(6) to dispose of the appeal within thirty days of its receipt, extendable by a further fifteen days for reasons to be recorded in writing, and a failure to dispose of the appeal within this period makes the First Appellate Authority liable for adverse comments in second appeal proceedings before the CIC. The first appeal is not merely a formal review of the CPIO's response but a substantive hearing at which the appellant may be invited to make oral or written submissions, and the First Appellate Authority must independently examine the information sought, the CPIO's response, the relevant records, and the applicable provisions of the Act before recording a reasoned order. The First Appellate Authority's order must be communicated in writing to the appellant and must contain the findings on each ground of appeal, the information ordered to be disclosed if the appeal is allowed in whole or in part, and the period within which such information is to be provided by the CPIO. In cases where the First Appellate Authority upholds the CPIO's refusal under one or more exemptions, the order must articulate the basis for finding the exemption applicable with sufficient particularity to enable the appellant to formulate a second appeal before the Central Information Commission. The Ministry of Defence's internal guidelines, circulated through the Joint Secretary (RTI) in Circular No. RTI-2/2021/D(RTI) dated 3rd September 2021, require the First Appellate Authority to maintain a register of first appeals, to acknowledge each appeal within three working days of receipt, and to ensure that the substantive disposal is completed well within the statutory period so as to minimize the number of second appeals filed before the CIC. The guidelines further direct that where the first appeal reveals a pattern of deficient RTI responses from a particular CPIO, the First Appellate Authority should initiate corrective training and supervisory measures rather than merely disposing of the individual appeal.

The second appeal to the Central Information Commission under Section 19(3) of the Act lies where a person is not satisfied with the order made by the First Appellate Authority within the Ministry of Defence, or where the First Appellate Authority has failed to make a decision within the prescribed period. The second appeal must be filed before the Central Information Commission in accordance with the Central Information Commission (Management) Regulations, 2007 and may be submitted in person, by post, or online through the CIC's designated portal. The Commission assigns the second appeal a registration number which serves as the permanent reference for all subsequent proceedings, and the Ministry of Defence maintains a dedicated CIC Coordination Cell within its RTI division to track all pending second appeals and to coordinate the preparation of responses and counter statements. Upon registration of a second appeal, the Commission issues notices to both the CPIO and the First Appellate Authority of the Ministry, along with a copy of the appeal, requiring them to submit a written reply within a specified period which is typically not less than three weeks. The CPIO's written reply must address each ground of appeal, restate the basis for any exemptions invoked, and if applicable, provide evidence that the statutory timelines were complied with. The Commission conducts hearings which may be physical, video-conferenced, or conducted on the papers where the parties consent, and the Ministry of Defence is typically represented by the CPIO and/or the First Appellate Authority, who may be accompanied by counsel on complex legal questions. The Central Information Commission is empowered under Section 18 and Section 19 of the Act to examine any record held by a public authority and no such record may be withheld from the Commission on any grounds whatsoever, which means that even classified files of the Ministry of Defence may be called for by the Commission for in camera examination. The Commission's power to impose penalty under Section 20(1) may be invoked at the stage of second appeal proceedings where it is established that the CPIO delayed or denied information without reasonable cause, and penalties imposed must be paid from personal funds and cannot be borne by the departmental budget. The Ministry of Defence has, in implementation of repeated CIC directions, established a system of recording compliance with CIC orders in a dedicated register maintained by the RTI Cell, and the status of compliance is reviewed at quarterly meetings chaired by the Joint Secretary (RTI). Where the Ministry disputes the correctness of a CIC order, it has the right to challenge the order before the High Court under Article 226 of the Constitution or under Section 23 of the Act which bars jurisdiction of civil courts but not High Courts, and such challenges are coordinated through the Ministry's legal cell in consultation with the Solicitor General of India where questions of significant constitutional or statutory importance are involved.

Every RTI response issued by the Central Public Information Officer of the Ministry of Defence must comply with the formal requirements prescribed under Section 7 of the Act and must contain certain mandatory elements to be regarded as a legally valid response. The response must be addressed to the applicant by name at the address provided in the application, must bear the RTI application reference number assigned by the CPIO's office, must cite the CPIO's file number in the standard format applicable to the concerned Division — for example, a response from the Department of Defence might bear the reference No. RTI/DoD/2024/1256 — and must be dated and signed by the CPIO personally or by an officer duly authorized under Section 5(4) of the Act. Where information is being provided, the response must specify the format in which information is being supplied, the fee for additional copies if payable, and a statement that the information provided is to the best of the CPIO's knowledge and belief accurate and complete as on the date of response. Where the CPIO is partially or wholly refusing a request, the response must cite the specific sub-section of Section 8(1) or other applicable provision under which the exemption is claimed, and must not merely state in general terms that the information is exempt without identifying the relevant legal provision with precision. The mandatory appeal information under Section 7(8)(i) and (ii) requires the response to contain the name, designation, and postal address of the First Appellate Authority to whom an appeal against the decision may be preferred, the period within which such appeal must be filed, and a statement that the applicant is entitled to approach the Central Information Commission in second appeal if dissatisfied with the First Appellate Authority's order. The language of the response should be in Hindi or English and, where the applicant has filed the application in an official regional language of the area, a response in that language is preferable, and the Ministry's RTI Manual notes that responses in bureaucratic jargon that are difficult for lay applicants to understand fail to meet the spirit of the Act even if they satisfy its formal requirements. Where a transfer of application has been effected under Section 6(3) because the information is held by another public authority, the response must clearly communicate the transfer to the applicant and provide the name and address of the public authority to which the application has been transferred. In the matter of fees for providing information, the CPIO must compute and communicate the fee payable for photocopies or inspection before supplying the information, and the response must specify that the thirty-day response period is suspended from the date of dispatch of the fee intimation until the date of receipt of the fee, as held by the Commission in CIC/MoD/A/2017/132456. The CIC has also directed that responses which merely acknowledge receipt or promise to provide information at a later date without specifying a definite date or amount of information do not satisfy the requirements of Section 7 and are to be treated as non-responses attracting the deemed refusal consequence.

The Union Budget presented to Parliament annually by the Finance Minister includes Defence as one of the major heads of expenditure, and the broad parameters of the Defence Budget — total allocation, division between capital and revenue expenditure, salaries and pensions as a proportion of revenue expenditure, and the broad category-wise breakdown — are publicly disclosed through the Budget documents laid before Parliament and available on the websites of the Ministry of Finance and Ministry of Defence. RTI applications seeking information about the Defence Budget are therefore answered in substantial part by reference to these publicly available documents, and the CPIO is entitled under Section 7(9) of the Act to direct the applicant to publicly available documents rather than physically supplying the information. However, there are several categories of defence financial information that attract exemptions — the detailed scheme-wise breakdown of the capital acquisition budget specifying the individual platforms and systems under procurement, the classified supplementary demands for specific strategic programmes, and the budget allocations for specific intelligence and special operations activities are typically withheld under Section 8(1)(a). The CIC has in CIC/MoD/A/2016/003412 held that aggregate expenditure on a completed and publicly acknowledged procurement project is not exempt once the project has moved past the sensitive negotiation stage, and directed the Ministry to disclose the total contract value of the procured systems. The suo motu disclosure obligations under Section 4(1)(b) of the Act require the Ministry of Defence to proactively publish on its website information including the budget allocated to each of its units and bodies, the planned expenditure, the actual expenditure, and the disbursements made, and the Ministry's Annual Report includes a chapter on financial performance which partially fulfils this obligation. The Commission in CIC/MoD/A/2019/164523 noted that the Ministry's suo motu disclosures on the website were incomplete in respect of the budgetary information required under Section 4(1)(b)(xi) and directed the Ministry to update the relevant section of the website within sixty days. Parliamentary budget documents being in the nature of legislative records are freely available and the CPIO may not charge fee for directing an applicant to them, but may charge the standard photocopying fee if the applicant requests physical copies of specific pages from the Ministry's own records of the Budget. The Ministry's internal budget files, noting sheets, internal approval processes, and correspondence with the Finance Ministry on budget negotiations are, however, generally protected from disclosure as they constitute part of the deliberative process protected under Section 8(1)(i), subject to the principle that once a decision has been taken, the decision and the material basis for it must be disclosed. Officers of the Ministry handling budget RTIs are instructed through the RTI Training Manual to distinguish carefully between pre-decisional budgetary deliberations and post-decisional budget information, as this distinction determines the applicable exemptions and the scope of permissible disclosure.

Section 4 of the Right to Information Act, 2005 imposes upon every public authority, including the Ministry of Defence and each of its constituent departments and attached offices, an obligation to proactively publish specified categories of information on their websites and through other modes of dissemination so as to minimise the need for citizens to file formal RTI applications. Section 4(1)(b) enumerates seventeen specific categories of information that must be published suo motu, including the particulars of the organization, functions, and duties of the authority; the powers and duties of its officers and employees; the procedure followed in decision-making, including channels of supervision and accountability; norms set for discharge of functions; rules, regulations, instructions, manuals and records held by the authority under its control; statements of the categories of documents held; and information pertaining to consultative processes for formulation of policy. The Ministry of Defence maintains a dedicated RTI portal at mod.gov.in which hosts the Section 4 disclosures for the Ministry Secretariat, and links to the Section 4 disclosures of the three Service Headquarters, DRDO, the Department of Ex-Servicemen Welfare, the Department of Defence Production, and other bodies are accessible through this portal. The compliance status of MoD with its Section 4 obligations has been the subject of periodic review by the Central Information Commission as well as by the Comptroller and Auditor General of India, and audits have found that while the Ministry's disclosures on organizational structure, rules, and service regulations are reasonably complete, disclosures on internal deliberative processes, contracts awarded, and citizen service delivery standards fall short of the requirements. The Ministry has been directed in multiple CIC orders, including the suo motu proceedings CIC/SS/2018/000123, to conduct a comprehensive audit of its Section 4 compliance and to submit an action taken report to the Commission within ninety days. The proactive disclosure of service rules and entitlement regulations is of particular importance to defence personnel and their families, and the Commission has noted in several orders that the non-availability of pension rules, disability benefit entitlements, and casualty compensation policy on the Ministry's website compels veterans to file RTI applications for information that should have been proactively published. Attached offices such as the Kendriya Sainik Board and Directorate General of Resettlement, which provide citizen-facing services to veterans and their families, are under a heightened obligation to publish service standards, application forms, eligibility criteria, and processing timelines under Section 4(1)(b)(xii) through (xv), and the Commission has noted incomplete compliance with these obligations in CIC/DESW/A/2020/114567 and directed remedial action. The Ministry issued a comprehensive action plan for Section 4 compliance upgradation through O.M. No. RTI-5/2022/D(RTI) dated 17th January 2022, assigning responsibility to each CPIO for updating and verifying their respective Section 4 disclosures on a quarterly basis and requiring the RTI Cell to maintain a compliance dashboard reflecting the current status of proactive disclosure across all RTI units under the Ministry.
