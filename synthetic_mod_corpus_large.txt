# MoD Synthetic Corpus - Extended Detail
# Topics: Hierarchy, Weapons, Budget, Legal, and Welfare

The Indian Army is headed by the Chief of the Army Staff (COAS), usually a four-star General. The operational structure consists of six operational commands and one training command (ARTRAC). The commands are Southern Command (Pune), Eastern Command (Kolkata), Central Command (Lucknow), Western Command (Chandimandir), Northern Command (Udhampur), and South-Western Command (Jaipur). Each command is led by a General Officer Commanding-in-Chief (GOC-in-C).

The Indian Navy, led by the Chief of the Naval Staff (CNS), is organized into three commands: Western Naval Command (Mumbai), Eastern Naval Command (Visakhapatnam), and Southern Naval Command (Kochi). The Navy's flagship is INS Vikrant, India's first indigenously built aircraft carrier. Submarine assets include the Arihant-class nuclear-powered ballistic missile submarines, emphasizing India's nuclear triad capability.

The Indian Air Force (IAF), under the Chief of the Air Staff (CAS), operates five operational commands, one maintenance command, and one training command. The IAF's combat fleet includes the Rafale, Su-30MKI, MiG-29, Mirage 2000, and the indigenous LCA Tejas. The induction of the S-400 Triumf air defence missile system has significantly enhanced India's long-range air defence capabilities.

The Chief of Defence Staff (CDS) office ensures the convergence of military strategic thought and procurement. The CDS is supported by the Department of Military Affairs (DMA) and oversees joint-service organizations like the Andaman and Nicobar Command (ANC) and the Strategic Forces Command (SFC).

Defence budget allocation for the 2024-25 fiscal year emphasized 'Aatmanirbharta'. Major budget heads include Category A for capital acquisitions (weapons and platforms) and Category B for revenue expenditure (salaries, rations, fuel). Under the 'Make in India' initiative, a significant portion of the capital budget is reserved for procurement from Indian industries.

The Right to Information (RTI) Act 2005 is widely used by defence personnel and the public. Under Section 8(1)(a) of the RTI Act, information which would prejudicially affect the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security, strategic, scientific or economic interests of the State, or relation with foreign state is exempt from disclosure. All units maintain a Central Public Information Officer (CPIO) and a First Appellate Authority (FAA) to process RTI requests within the mandated 30-day timeline.

The Cantonment Act 2006 governs the administration of the 62 cantonments across India. Cantonment Boards are autonomous bodies under the Ministry of Defence that provide civic administration and ensure the maintenance of infrastructure in military areas. The Station Commander typically serves as the President of the Cantonment Board, while a Chief Executive Officer (CEO) oversees civilian administration.

The Soldier's welfare is managed through various agencies. The Kendriya Sainik Board (KSB) is the apex body for the welfare of ex-servicemen and their families. It manages the Armed Forces Flag Day Fund, which provides financial assistance to widows, disabled soldiers, and for the education of dependants. The Ex-Servicemen Contributory Health Scheme (ECHS) provides cashless medical coverage through a network of polyclinics and empanelled private hospitals, ensuring health security for retirees.

The Agnipath scheme facilitates the induction of youth as Agniveers for a four-year period. Agniveers are trained in various military skills and technologies. Upon completion of their stint, they receive the Seva Nidhi package, which is exempt from Income Tax. This scheme contributes to a leaner and more tech-savvy force.

The Defence Acquisition Council (DAC) is responsible for the overall procurement policy. It approves the List of Indigenized Items (Positive Indigenization Lists), which bans the import of specific items to promote local manufacturing. The list has been expanded to include hundreds of items, ranging from simple spares to complex electronic warfare systems.

Major training institutions include:
1. National Defence Academy (NDA), Khadakwasla: Common training for all three services.
2. Indian Military Academy (IMA), Dehradun: For officers of the Indian Army.
3. Officers Training Academy (OTA), Chennai and Gaya: For Short Service Commission (SSC).
4. Indian Naval Academy (INA), Ezhimala: For Naval officers.
5. Air Force Academy (AFA), Dundigal: For IAF pilots and ground staff.

Pay and allowances follow the 7th Central Pay Commission (CPC) recommendations. Military Service Pay (MSP) is a special allowance provided to recognize the unique hardships of military life. Allowances such as the Siachen Allowance, Field Area Allowance, and High Altitude Allowance are paid based on the location and nature of the duty.

Promotions of officers are based on a 'Selection Board' system from the rank of Colonel onwards. The evaluation includes Annual Confidential Reports (ACRs), course results, and performance in key command and staff appointments. The process is designed to select the most capable leaders for higher command responsibilities.
